# Research Plan: Transfer Sign Heterogeneity in Multi-Task Learning

**Working title:** *When Task Affinity Changes Sign: Transfer Sign Heterogeneity in Multi-Task Learning*

**Alternate title:** *Global Task Affinity Can Be Misleading: Transfer Sign Heterogeneity in Molecular Multi-Task Learning*

---

## Part 1: Core Thesis and Pre-Commitments

### 1.1 Central claim

Multi-task learning methods typically treat task compatibility as a global relationship between task pairs. We show this assumption can fail: the same pair of prediction tasks can exhibit positive transfer in one region of input space and negative transfer in another. This *transfer sign heterogeneity* causes global task-affinity scores to cancel opposing effects and mislead task grouping. Region-conditional gradient affinity, computed under explicit local-support constraints, predicts where multi-task training helps and where it hurts.

The slogan version: **task affinity is scalar only when transfer is regionally stable.**

### 1.2 Contribution stack

The paper's contribution is not a single method or a single component. It is a coherent package of phenomenon, failure mode, diagnostic, and utility:

| Contribution | What it establishes | How it is evaluated |
|---|---|---|
| **Phenomenon** | Transfer sign heterogeneity exists: the same task pair can show positive and negative transfer across input regions | Prevalence across datasets exceeds random-partition null |
| **Failure mode** | Global affinity cancellation: low global affinity often hides large opposing regional effects | Cancellation index distribution across task pairs |
| **Diagnostic** | Region-conditional gradient affinity predicts regional transfer beyond chemistry baselines | Incremental $R^2$ over scaffold similarity and label correlation |
| **Utility** | Support-aware share/separate/abstain decisions reduce regional negative transfer | Comparison against MMoE, adaptive sharing, and global grouping baselines |

Individual components (gradient affinity, scaffolds, bootstrap, regions, MTL) are not individually novel. The contribution is the claim stack: that task affinity is region-conditional and sign-changing, that this causes global affinity to mislead, that local gradient affinity is a useful diagnostic for it, and that this diagnostic supports actionable sharing decisions.

### 1.2 What this paper does NOT claim

To preempt scoop accusations and overclaiming, the paper explicitly does not claim:

- That gradient-based task affinity is novel (TAG, Fifty et al.)
- That input-conditional sharing is novel (DynaShare, MMoE, Cross-Stitch, Sluice)
- That task grouping is novel (Standley et al.)
- That global task affinity is always invalid
- That local routing architectures are novel
- That sample-overlap requirements for gradient affinity are new; we generalize the principle from global sample overlap (your ICLR paper) to regional support-validity

The protected novelty is the explicit identification, measurement, and prediction of transfer sign heterogeneity within task pairs across regions of input space, plus the demonstration that this regional structure is actionable for sharing decisions.

### 1.3 Methodological pre-commitments

These choices are locked before the pilot. Changing them post-hoc would constitute p-hacking and the plan must commit now.

**Affinity estimator:** Cosine similarity of region-restricted gradients $G_{ij}(r) = \cos(g_i(r), g_j(r))$ as the primary metric. TAG-style update-affinity (effect of one task's gradient step on another's loss) is the secondary metric for ablation only. Rationale: cosine similarity is what your ICLR paper uses, so the comparison to global affinity is apples-to-apples; TAG-style affinity is computationally heavier and adding it as primary changes the project scope.

**MTL training protocol:** Train pairwise MTL models on the full data distribution, evaluate per region. Train STL models on full data, evaluate per region. Regional MTL benefit measured both as pair-average and as task-specific (see Section 2.3). Rationale: per-region training has insufficient sample size for stable comparisons; the joint-training-regional-evaluation setup is the right operationalization for the claim "the MTL model trained on the joint distribution exhibits region-conditional benefit."

The paper must explicitly frame the claim in terms of joint-trained models. The claim is not "training MTL on region B alone would hurt." It is "an MTL model trained on the full distribution exhibits negative transfer when evaluated on region B."

**Region partitioning:** Bemis-Murcko scaffolds as primary, latent embedding clusters (k-means on encoder representations) as robustness check, kNN neighborhoods as secondary robustness check. The phenomenon must hold under at least scaffolds + one of the other two. Random partitions, matched for region size, serve as a properly calibrated negative control with explicit null distribution.

**Bootstrap procedure:** Hierarchical bootstrap. Resample scaffold clusters (or embedding clusters) with replacement, then resample compounds within each resampled cluster. Naive iid bootstrap will not be used because compounds within scaffolds are not independent.

**Statistical thresholds:** Effect-size based. A region exhibits meaningful regional benefit if $|\Delta_{ij}(r)| > 1.5$ AUC points (classification) or 0.10 RMSE (regression) AND the hierarchical-bootstrap 95% CI does not cross zero. A task pair exhibits sign heterogeneity if it has at least one region with $\Delta_{ij}(r) > +1.5$ and at least one with $\Delta_{ij}(r) < -1.5$, both passing the CI test.

**Definition of "meaningful task pair":** A task pair counts as meaningful (and thus enters the heterogeneity-prevalence denominator) only if all four conditions hold:

1. At least 3 valid regions after local-support filtering ($n_{\min} = 50$ per task)
2. At least one region with $|\Delta_{ij}(r)| > 1.5$ AUC and CI not crossing zero
3. Non-degenerate labels per region for classification (at least 5 positive and 5 negative examples per task per region)
4. Regional test set size $\geq 30$ for classification or $\geq 50$ for regression

This locked definition prevents denominator games during analysis.

## Part 2: Mathematical Framework and Methodology

This section is intended to be precise enough that an engineer (human or LLM) can implement it directly. Every quantity, hyperparameter, and procedural choice is specified.

### 2.1 Notation

- $T = \{t_1, \ldots, t_K\}$: tasks (e.g., the 12 endpoints of Tox21)
- $\mathcal{X}$: molecular input space (SMILES strings, parsed to graphs)
- $D_i \subseteq \mathcal{X}$: training compounds with valid labels for task $i$
- $V_i \subseteq \mathcal{X}$: validation compounds for task $i$
- $T_i \subseteq \mathcal{X}$: test compounds for task $i$
- All splits are stratified by label (where applicable) AND by scaffold (compounds sharing a Bemis-Murcko scaffold cannot appear in both train and test)
- $\mathcal{R} = \{r_1, \ldots, r_M\}$: input-space partition into M regions, induced by a partitioning function $\pi: \mathcal{X} \to \{1, \ldots, M\}$
- $D_i(r) = \{x \in D_i : \pi(x) = r\}$: task $i

## Part 3: Risk Register and Mitigations

### Risk 1 (PRIMARY SCIENTIFIC RISK): Scaffold similarity explains everything

**Probability:** Medium-high. Two regions in the same scaffold cluster are by definition chemically similar; this might fully explain regional transfer patterns and reduce the gradient-affinity contribution to noise.

**Why this is the primary risk:** If true, the paper has no methodological contribution. The phenomenon would still exist, but the predictor's added value over a chemistry baseline would be zero, which would force a workshop paper.

**Detection:** In Phase 2, run joint regression $\Delta_{ij}(r) \sim \text{ScaffoldSim}_{ij}(r) + G_{ij}(r) + \text{LabelCorr}_{ij}(r)$ and report incremental $R^2$ and partial correlation of each predictor. This is a centerpiece result, not an aside.

**Mitigation:**
- Latent embedding partitioning as alternative (decouples chemical similarity from learned task structure)
- Within-scaffold-cluster analysis showing $G_{ij}(r)$ varies meaningfully even when scaffold structure is held constant
- Report incremental $R^2$ for $G_{ij}(r)$ over chemistry baselines as a primary result
- Consider that gradient affinity may capture *task-specific* representational alignment that scaffold similarity cannot, especially across diverse molecular families

**Kill criterion:** $G_{ij}(r)$ contributes less than $0.05$ incremental $R^2$ over scaffold similarity in joint regression, across multiple datasets. Reframe paper around scaffold-based regional structure rather than gradient affinity, and pivot to workshop submission.

### Risk 2: Sign heterogeneity is rare or absent

**Probability:** Medium. Plausible that MTL benefit is roughly monotonic across regions for most pairs.

**Detection:** Pilot Phase 0 directly measures prevalence.

**Mitigation:**
- Test deliberately weakly-related task pairs (cross-domain Tox21+ADME) where heterogeneity is more likely than for tightly-related pairs
- Test high-overlap datasets first to remove confounds
- Define meaningful effect sizes upfront (the 1.5 AUC threshold)
- Use sufficient region partitioning resolution (3-8 regions per dataset)
- Examine task-specific (asymmetric) heterogeneity as a separate channel — even pairs that look symmetric may show asymmetric regional transfer

**Kill criterion:** Fewer than 5 task pairs across all datasets show stable sign heterogeneity passing the CI test. Project becomes a workshop paper or is dropped.

### Risk 3: Local gradient affinity does not predict regional transfer sign

**Probability:** Medium-low. Your ICLR paper showed global cosine works above 30% overlap; locally restricted versions should also work given enough samples per region.

**Detection:** Phase 0 pilot measures regional sign prediction AUROC.

**Mitigation:**
- TAG-style affinity as backup metric (pre-registered as ablation)
- Last-shared-layer gradients vs. all-encoder gradients (tested as ablation)
- Fisher-normalized gradients (tested as ablation)
- Larger region sizes if small-region noise dominates

**Kill criterion:** Local gradient affinity AUROC for regional sign prediction does not exceed best non-gradient baseline (scaffold similarity, regional label correlation, embedding distance) by at least 0.07. Project drops to workshop paper.

### Risk 4: Reviewers see this as DynaShare/MMoE rebranded

**Probability:** Medium. The most likely reviewer attack.

**Mitigation:**
- Frame the contribution as diagnosis and measurement, not as a competing architecture
- Include MMoE and one other adaptive sharing baseline (Cross-Stitch, Sluice, or DynaShare if implementation is clean) as performance baselines
- Show calibrated regional sign prediction as something routing methods do not provide (their gating weights are not designed to predict regional MTL benefit)
- Demonstrate that local gradient affinity, computed before MTL training, predicts what routing methods learn during training. This positions local affinity as a *predictor* and *diagnostic*, not a competitor

### Risk 5: Region partitioning is itself a confound

**Probability:** Medium. Different partitionings might give different sign-heterogeneity prevalence.

**Mitigation:**
- Test all three partitioning schemes (scaffolds, latent clusters, kNN)
- Report agreement: phenomenon must hold across at least two
- Random-partition negative control with proper null distribution: scaffold-based prevalence must exceed 95th percentile of random-partition null
- Vary number of regions (3, 5, 8, 10) and report stability

### Risk 6: Small-region statistical noise

**Probability:** High. Some regions will have small samples even after the $n_{\min}$ threshold.

**Mitigation:**
- Hierarchical bootstrap CIs everywhere
- Abstention via the CI-width criterion
- Multiple seeds (5 final) and averaging
- Explicit noise-floor experiment: shuffle labels within regions and show $G_{ij}(r)$ becomes noise

### Risk 7: Compute cost

**Probability:** Medium. Pairwise MTL training across many task pairs, multiple seeds, multiple architectures is expensive.

**Mitigation:**
- 2-4 H100 GPUs per available tooling
- Reuse trained encoders where possible
- Pilot uses 3 seeds; final uses 5
- Limit final task-pair count to ~80-120 across all datasets
- Stage compute: don't run final architecture variants until pilot greenlights

### Risk 8: Phenomenon exists but is too dataset-specific

**Probability:** Medium-low. Phenomenon should generalize across molecular datasets, but might not generalize beyond chemistry.

**Mitigation:**
- Own the molecular scope explicitly. The paper is about sparse molecular MTL with chemical structure.
- Briefly speculate on generalizability in discussion without claiming it
- Future work section discusses extension to other domains

## Part 4: Pre-Pilot Probability Estimates

Before any experiments are run, the unconditional probability estimates are:

- Phenomenon exists at green-light prevalence: 40-55%
- Local gradient affinity passes predictor green-light: 50-65% conditional on phenomenon existing
- Both pass: 25-35%
- Scaffold similarity does NOT explain everything: 55-70% conditional on phenomenon existing
- NeurIPS main acceptance conditional on all checks passing: 35-45%
- NeurIPS main acceptance unconditional: 12-18%
- Workshop acceptance unconditional: 70-80%

These are honest estimates. The unconditional NeurIPS probability is in the 12-18% range, which sounds low but reflects the compounding risk of pilot failure, scaffold-similarity confound, execution issues, and reviewer variance. If the pilot greenlights, the conditional probability rises substantially.

## Part 5: Phase 0 — Pilot

### 5.1 Goal

Decide whether the project is worth pursuing. Test the phenomenon claim and the predictor claim with minimal infrastructure.

### 5.2 Datasets

Tox21 (12 tasks, 100% overlap), SIDER (27 tasks, 100% overlap). High overlap removes the support-availability confound at this stage.

### 5.3 Task pairs

20-40 task pairs sampled to include:
- Within-mechanism pairs (e.g., NR vs. NR pathways in Tox21) — expected positive transfer
- Cross-mechanism pairs (NR vs. SR) — likely heterogeneous
- SOC-grouped vs. cross-SOC pairs in SIDER

### 5.4 Architecture

Reuse ICLR paper's GCN architecture: 3-layer GCN, hidden dim 256, global mean pooling, MLP heads. AdamW, lr 1e-3, batch 32, 30 epochs, early stopping on validation.

### 5.5 Region partitioning

Bemis-Murcko scaffolds. Cluster scaffolds into 3-5 groups using hierarchical clustering on Tanimoto similarity of scaffold fingerprints. Require minimum 50 compounds per region.

### 5.6 Experimental protocol

For each dataset:

1. Train STL models for each task. 3 seeds. Save checkpoints.
2. Train pairwise MTL models for each task pair. 3 seeds. Save checkpoints.
3. For each pair × region: compute $\Delta_{ij}(r)$, $\Delta_{i \leftarrow j}(r)$, $\Delta_{j \leftarrow i}(r)$ on test split with hierarchical-bootstrap 95% CI.
4. For each pair × region: compute $G_{ij}(r)$ from gradients on training compounds in region. Hierarchical-bootstrap CI on $G_{ij}(r)$.
5. For each pair: compute $S_{ij}$ (sign heterogeneity, pair and task-specific), $H_{ij}$ (heterogeneity index), $C_{ij}$ (cancellation).
6. Test predictor: across all valid regions, does $G_{ij}(r)$ predict $\text{sign}(\Delta_{ij}(r))$? Compute AUROC and Spearman.
7. Build random-partition null distributions (200 random partitions per dataset) and compare scaffold-based metrics against the null.
8. Filter task pairs by the four-criteria "meaningful pair" definition before computing prevalence.

### 5.7 Green-light criteria (BOTH required)

**Phenomenon check:**
- At least 5 task pairs across the two datasets show stable sign heterogeneity (passes $|\Delta| > 1.5$ AUC threshold AND CI test in at least one positive and one negative region)
- AND at least 15% of meaningful task pairs (per the four-criteria definition in Section 1.3) exhibit sign heterogeneity
- AND scaffold-based heterogeneity prevalence exceeds the 95th percentile of the random-partition null distribution

**Predictor check:**
- Local gradient affinity AUROC for regional sign prediction $\geq 0.70$
- AND local gradient affinity AUROC exceeds best non-gradient baseline (scaffold similarity, regional label correlation, embedding distance) by at least 0.07
- AND Spearman $\rho$ between $G_{ij}(r)$ and $\Delta_{ij}(r) \geq 0.45$
- AND results stable across 3 seeds (sign of $G_{ij}(r)$ agrees in $\geq 80\%$ of regions across seeds)

### 5.8 Decision tree

- **Both pass:** Full project proceed. NeurIPS target.
- **Phenomenon passes, predictor fails:** Workshop paper on phenomenon discovery; or invest in better predictors (TAG-style, Fisher-normalized) before deciding.
- **Phenomenon fails, predictor untestable:** Drop project or pivot to "conditions under which global affinity is sufficient" framing (much weaker, probably workshop only).
- **Both fail:** Drop project.

### 5.9 Pilot deliverables

- Sign heterogeneity prevalence table across datasets, with random-partition null comparison
- Pair-averaged AND task-specific regional benefit tables
- Predictor AUROC comparison vs. baselines
- One compelling task-pair example for Figure 2
- Hierarchical-bootstrap stability assessment
- Decision document with explicit go/no-go reasoning

## Part 7: Phase 2 — Predictive Evaluation

### 7.1 Goal

Show that local gradient affinity predicts regional transfer better than alternatives, and crucially that it adds value beyond chemistry baselines.

### 7.2 Prediction targets

For each (task pair, region) with valid local support:
- Binary: $\text{sign}(\Delta_{ij}(r))$
- Continuous: $\Delta_{ij}(r)$ value
- Three-class: positive transfer / negative transfer / no significant transfer
- Task-specific equivalents: $\text{sign}(\Delta_{i \leftarrow j}(r))$ and $\text{sign}(\Delta_{j \leftarrow i}(r))$

### 7.3 Predictor methods

**Primary methods:**
1. Local gradient cosine: $G_{ij}(r)$
2. Local gradient cosine + local support size as features (small classifier)
3. TAG-style local affinity: effect of one task's regional gradient step on the other task's regional loss

**Non-gradient baselines:**
4. Scaffold similarity: mean Tanimoto similarity between $D_i(r)$ and $D_j(r)$ scaffolds
5. Regional embedding distance: $\|\text{mean}(\phi(D_i(r))) - \text{mean}(\phi(D_j(r)))\|$
6. Regional label correlation: Pearson correlation of labels for compounds with both labels in $r$ (when available)
7. Regional MMD between task input distributions
8. Global gradient affinity: $G_{ij}^{\text{global}}$ from your ICLR paper, applied to all regions of a pair (constant predictor)
9. Regional Task2Vec embedding similarity (if computationally feasible)

**Joint predictor (centerpiece for primary risk):**
10. Logistic regression: $\text{sign}(\Delta_{ij}(r)) \sim G_{ij}(r) + \text{ScaffoldSim} + \text{LabelCorr} + \text{EmbeddingDist}$ — used to compute incremental contribution of $G_{ij}(r)$ over chemistry baselines

### 7.4 Metrics

- AUROC for sign prediction (pair-averaged AND task-specific)
- AUPRC
- Balanced accuracy
- Spearman $\rho$ with $\Delta_{ij}(r)$
- Calibration (reliability diagram, ECE)
- Hierarchical-bootstrap CIs on all metrics
- **Incremental $R^2$ of $G_{ij}(r)$ in joint regression — primary scientific result**

### 7.5 Cross-dataset transfer

Train predictor on Tox21, evaluate on SIDER. Train on SIDER, evaluate on ToxCast. Tests whether the relationship between local gradient affinity and regional transfer generalizes across datasets.

### 7.6 Phase 2 deliverables

- Main predictor comparison table (Figure 5)
- Scatter plot of $G_{ij}(r)$ vs. $\Delta_{ij}(r)$ across all datasets (Figure 4)
- Incremental $R^2$ figure showing $G_{ij}(r)$ adds predictive value beyond chemistry baselines (Figure 5b — centerpiece for primary risk)
- Calibration plot for top predictors
- Cross-dataset transfer results

## Part 8: Phase 3 — Utility Evaluation

This phase establishes practical value: using local affinity to make sharing decisions. Real architectures, real baselines, honest comparison.

### 8.1 Goal

Show that local-affinity-guided sharing reduces regional negative transfer while preserving regional positive transfer, and either matches or beats existing routing methods.

### 8.2 Sharing strategies compared

**Single-task baselines:**
1. STL: train each task independently

**Static all-task baselines:**
2. All-task MTL: train one shared encoder on all tasks
3. Pairwise MTL union: train pairwise MTL for each pair, ensemble at test

**Global grouping baselines:**
4. Random grouping (10 trials averaged)
5. Global gradient affinity grouping (your ICLR paper)
6. TAG-style affinity grouping (Fifty et al. or close re-implementation)

**Adaptive sharing baselines:**
7. MMoE: mixture-of-experts with task-specific gates
8. One additional adaptive sharing method: DynaShare if implementation is clean and faithful, otherwise Cross-Stitch Networks (Misra et al.) or Sluice Networks (Ruder et al.). Decision made before Phase 3 begins based on engineering assessment. Bad implementation is worse than no implementation; the baseline must be defensible.

**Local affinity strategies (the proposed approach):**
9. Local affinity hard sharing: For each region, pre-compute $G_{ij}(r)$ for all task pairs. Train region-conditional MTL where shared parameters are active only between task pairs with $G_{ij}(r) > \tau_+$ in that region.
10. Local affinity soft sharing: Use $G_{ij}(r)$ as a continuous gate weight on cross-task parameter sharing per region.
11. Local affinity with abstention: Like (9), but for low-support regions, default to STL prediction.

### 8.3 Implementation of local affinity sharing architecture

The proposed architecture must be implemented carefully. One reasonable approach:

- Shared encoder produces representation $\phi(x)$
- Region assignment function $r(x)$ assigns each compound to a region (scaffold-based, deterministic)
- Task heads receive $\phi(x)$ plus region identity
- For each task pair $(i, j)$ and region $r$, a learned binary gate $\gamma_{ij}(r)$ controls whether encoder gradients from task $i$ flow back through shared parameters when task $j$ is being trained on region $r$
- $\gamma_{ij}(r)$ is initialized using $G_{ij}(r)$ pre-computed from a short pretraining run, then optionally fine-tuned

This is a significant engineering effort. The paper includes a clean public implementation.

### 8.4 Datasets

- Tox21, SIDER, ToxCast (high overlap)
- TDC ADMET, kinase panel (sparse)

### 8.5 Metrics

**Performance metrics:**
- Average per-task AUC/RMSE (across tasks)
- Worst-region performance (per task, then averaged)
- Average regional performance
- Sample-weighted average performance

**Negative transfer metrics:**
- Negative transfer rate: fraction of (task, region) pairs where method underperforms STL by at least 1.5 AUC
- Severe negative transfer rate: underperforms by at least 3 AUC
- Average regional negative transfer magnitude
- Task-specific negative transfer rate (using $\Delta_{i \leftarrow j}$ rather than pair average)

**Decision-quality metrics:**
- Calibration of share/separate decisions: when method predicts "share is beneficial," how often is it correct?
- Reliability diagrams for share/separate predictions
- Expected calibration error (ECE) on share/separate decisions
- Held-out task-pair calibration: fit decision rule on training task pairs, evaluate calibration on held-out pairs
- AUROC of sharing decisions vs. ground-truth regional benefit

**Efficiency metrics:**
- Training compute (GPU-hours)
- Inference latency
- Number of effective parameters

### 8.6 Statistical comparison

Pairwise comparisons between methods using paired tests across (task, region) combinations. Bonferroni correction across the comparison family. Hierarchical-bootstrap CIs on all reported numbers.

### 8.7 Expected outcomes and pre-committed interpretation

- If local affinity sharing matches or beats DynaShare/MMoE on average performance AND has substantially lower negative transfer rate: strong result
- If local affinity sharing matches DynaShare/MMoE on average but is interpretable while they are not: moderate result, framed around interpretability and calibration
- If local affinity sharing substantially underperforms DynaShare/MMoE on average: still publishable as a diagnostic, but the contribution shifts

The paper must commit to honesty here. If routing methods beat local affinity on raw performance, the paper says so and reframes the contribution around regional diagnosis and calibrated abstention rather than claiming SOTA.

### 8.8 Phase 3 deliverables

- Main performance table across all methods and datasets
- Negative transfer rate comparison (key figure)
- Calibration analysis with reliability diagrams
- Per-region performance breakdown for selected task pairs
- Public implementation of local-affinity sharing architecture

## Part 9: Phase 4 — Robustness and Ablations

### 9.1 Region partitioning robustness

For each dataset, test:
- Bemis-Murcko scaffolds (primary)
- Latent embedding clusters with k = 3, 5, 8, 10
- kNN neighborhoods
- Random partitions (negative control with full null distribution)

Phenomenon must hold across at least scaffolds + one of latent/kNN. Random partitions should produce heterogeneity prevalence below the 5th percentile of scaffold-based prevalence.

### 9.2 Sample size thresholds

Vary $n_{\min}$:
- 25, 50, 100, 150

Show:
- Phenomenon prevalence as a function of $n_{\min}$
- Predictor AUROC as a function of $n_{\min}$
- Abstention rate increases as $n_{\min}$ increases
- Retained accuracy (excluding abstained regions) is stable or improves

### 9.3 Architecture robustness

Repeat key experiments with:
- GCN (primary)
- GAT
- Molecular transformer (ChemBERTa or similar) if compute allows
- ECFP+MLP (fixed-feature baseline)

For each architecture, report:
- Sign heterogeneity prevalence
- Predictor AUROC
- Whether the specific regions identified as positive/negative agree across architectures (Jaccard similarity of "positive transfer regions" across architectures)

Your ICLR paper showed gradient patterns are consistent across learned architectures (r=0.71-0.81) and differ for ECFP. Expect similar pattern here.

### 9.4 Seed robustness

5 seeds for final results. Report standard errors and CIs. Sign agreement across seeds for the heterogeneity indicator.

### 9.5 Gradient computation ablations

- Gradients on last shared encoder layer only vs. all encoder layers
- Cosine similarity vs. dot product (unnormalized)
- Fisher-normalized gradients
- Mean gradient over training trajectory vs. final-checkpoint gradient

### 9.6 Negative controls

- Random label permutation: shuffle task labels within each region. $G_{ij}(r)$ should drop to noise.
- Random region assignment: matched-size random partitions, full null distribution. Should not produce sign heterogeneity at the rate observed under scaffolds.
- Single-task identity check: $G_{ii}(r) \approx 1$ for all $r$, validating the metric.

### 9.7 Number of regions

Vary M = 3, 5, 8, 10. Heterogeneity prevalence and predictor AUROC should be relatively stable; sharp dependence would suggest overfitting to partition.

## Part 10: Phase 5 — Theory and Discussion

### 10.1 Theoretical contributions

The paper does not claim a major theorem but includes:

- A formal definition of region-conditional task affinity
- A statement (with proof sketch in appendix) that under structured covariate shift across regions, the global gradient affinity is a function of (a) regional affinities, (b) regional sample weights, (c) the cosine geometry — and is not in general a weighted average of regional affinities
- A signal-to-noise analysis of when $G_{ij}(r)$ is a reliable estimator: depends on per-region sample size, gradient norm, and noise structure
- Conditions under which local-affinity-guided sharing is provably beneficial in expectation (under simplified assumptions)

These are framed as supporting analysis, not primary contributions. The paper is empirical.

### 10.2 Connection to ICLR paper

Explicit section showing the ICLR paper's overlap requirement is a special case. When tasks have full overlap (Tox21, SIDER), the global gradient affinity is reliable because all compounds are shared. When overlap is partial, regions of the input space have asymmetric task coverage, and global affinity becomes a function of which regions happen to be sampled. The NeurIPS paper generalizes the ICLR result by showing that even at full overlap, regional structure can make global affinity misleading. We generalize the principle from global sample overlap to regional support-validity.

### 10.3 When global affinity is sufficient

The paper explicitly identifies regimes where global affinity is fine:
- High-overlap datasets where all task pairs have positive (or all negative) transfer everywhere
- Task pairs where regional effects are roughly uniform
- Coarse-grained planning where regional decisions are not actionable

This honest framing strengthens the paper.

### 10.4 Limitations

- Molecular focus: extension to vision/NLP is future work
- Region partitioning is itself a modeling choice; the paper doesn't fully solve "what is the right region structure"
- Local-affinity sharing architecture has computational overhead vs. all-task MTL
- Regional sign prediction requires sufficient per-region samples; very sparse settings remain challenging
- Hierarchical bootstrap assumes scaffold groupings have approximately exchangeable substructure within clusters

## Part 12: Paper Structure

### 12.1 Abstract

Multi-task learning methods typically treat task compatibility as a global relationship between task pairs. We show this assumption can fail: the same pair of molecular prediction tasks can exhibit positive transfer in one chemical region and negative transfer in another. This *transfer sign heterogeneity* causes global task-affinity scores to cancel opposing regional effects and mislead task grouping. We define region-conditional transfer benefit, introduce support-valid regional gradient affinity as a diagnostic, and show that local gradient affinity predicts regional transfer beyond scaffold similarity and label-correlation baselines. Across five molecular datasets, sign heterogeneity is common (X% of meaningful task pairs, exceeding random-partition null at p<0.05) and produces measurable cancellation in global affinity. Local gradient affinity predicts regional transfer sign with AUROC Y, adding incremental $R^2$ of Z over chemistry baselines. Using local affinity for share/separate/abstain decisions reduces regional negative transfer by W% while matching adaptive sharing on average performance. Our results suggest task affinity should not be treated as a scalar relationship in domains where transfer is regionally heterogeneous.

### 12.2 Introduction (1.5 pages)

Lead with the phenomenon, not the method:

- MTL needs task compatibility decisions. Existing methods (TAG, DynaShare, MMoE) provide task-pair-level affinity scores or implicit instance-level routing.
- These methods share an unstated assumption: task compatibility is a property of the task pair. We show this assumption can fail.
- Concrete example: introduce one canonical sign-heterogeneous task pair from results, with the regional benefit figure inline.
- Generalization: across five datasets, this happens often enough that global task affinity is systematically misleading.
- The ICLR paper showed gradient affinity requires shared samples. We show that even with shared samples, the same pair can show opposite transfer in different regions, generalizing the principle from sample overlap to regional support-validity.
- Contribution stack:
  1. **Phenomenon**: define and measure transfer sign heterogeneity (pair-averaged and task-specific)
  2. **Failure mode**: characterize global affinity cancellation
  3. **Diagnostic**: region-conditional gradient affinity with explicit support-validity, evaluated against chemistry baselines
  4. **Utility**: local-affinity sharing reduces negative transfer
  5. **Robustness**: phenomenon holds across architectures, partitionings, and sample sizes
- Field-level implication: task compatibility is sign-changing, support-dependent, and region-conditional. Single-scalar task affinity is sufficient only when transfer is regionally stable.

## Part 14: Honest Bottom Line

## Part 3: Risk Register and Mitigations

### Risk 1 (PRIMARY SCIENTIFIC RISK): Scaffold similarity explains everything

**Probability:** Medium-high. Two regions in the same scaffold cluster are by definition chemically similar; this might fully explain regional transfer patterns and reduce the gradient-affinity contribution to noise.

**Why this is the primary risk:** If true, the paper has no methodological contribution. The phenomenon would still exist, but the predictor's added value over a chemistry baseline would be zero, which would force a workshop paper.

**Detection:** In Phase 2, run joint regression $\Delta_{ij}(r) \sim \text{ScaffoldSim}_{ij}(r) + G_{ij}(r) + \text{LabelCorr}_{ij}(r)$ and report incremental $R^2$ and partial correlation of each predictor. This is a centerpiece result, not an aside.

**Mitigation:**
- Latent embedding partitioning as alternative (decouples chemical similarity from learned task structure)
- Within-scaffold-cluster analysis showing $G_{ij}(r)$ varies meaningfully even when scaffold structure is held constant
- Report incremental $R^2$ for $G_{ij}(r)$ over chemistry baselines as a primary result
- Consider that gradient affinity may capture *task-specific* representational alignment that scaffold similarity cannot, especially across diverse molecular families

**Kill criterion:** $G_{ij}(r)$ contributes less than $0.05$ incremental $R^2$ over scaffold similarity in joint regression, across multiple datasets. Reframe paper around scaffold-based regional structure rather than gradient affinity, and pivot to workshop submission.

### Risk 2: Sign heterogeneity is rare or absent

**Probability:** Medium. Plausible that MTL benefit is roughly monotonic across regions for most pairs.

**Detection:** Pilot Phase 0 directly measures prevalence.

**Mitigation:**
- Test deliberately weakly-related task pairs (cross-domain Tox21+ADME) where heterogeneity is more likely than for tightly-related pairs
- Test high-overlap datasets first to remove confounds
- Define meaningful effect sizes upfront (the 1.5 AUC threshold)
- Use sufficient region partitioning resolution (3-8 regions per dataset)
- Examine task-specific (asymmetric) heterogeneity as a separate channel — even pairs that look symmetric may show asymmetric regional transfer

**Kill criterion:** Fewer than 5 task pairs across all datasets show stable sign heterogeneity passing the CI test. Project becomes a workshop paper or is dropped.

### Risk 3: Local gradient affinity does not predict regional transfer sign

**Probability:** Medium-low. Your ICLR paper showed global cosine works above 30% overlap; locally restricted versions should also work given enough samples per region.

**Detection:** Phase 0 pilot measures regional sign prediction AUROC.

**Mitigation:**
- TAG-style affinity as backup metric (pre-registered as ablation)
- Last-shared-layer gradients vs. all-encoder gradients (tested as ablation)
- Fisher-normalized gradients (tested as ablation)
- Larger region sizes if small-region noise dominates

**Kill criterion:** Local gradient affinity AUROC for regional sign prediction does not exceed best non-gradient baseline (scaffold similarity, regional label correlation, embedding distance) by at least 0.07. Project drops to workshop paper.

### Risk 4: Reviewers see this as DynaShare/MMoE rebranded

**Probability:** Medium. The most likely reviewer attack.

**Mitigation:**
- Frame the contribution as diagnosis and measurement, not as a competing architecture
- Include MMoE and one other adaptive sharing baseline (Cross-Stitch, Sluice, or DynaShare if implementation is clean) as performance baselines
- Show calibrated regional sign prediction as something routing methods do not provide (their gating weights are not designed to predict regional MTL benefit)
- Demonstrate that local gradient affinity, computed before MTL training, predicts what routing methods learn during training. This positions local affinity as a *predictor* and *diagnostic*, not a competitor

### Risk 5: Region partitioning is itself a confound

**Probability:** Medium. Different partitionings might give different sign-heterogeneity prevalence.

**Mitigation:**
- Test all three partitioning schemes (scaffolds, latent clusters, kNN)
- Report agreement: phenomenon must hold across at least two
- Random-partition negative control with proper null distribution: scaffold-based prevalence must exceed 95th percentile of random-partition null
- Vary number of regions (3, 5, 8, 10) and report stability

### Risk 6: Small-region statistical noise

**Probability:** High. Some regions will have small samples even after the $n_{\min}$ threshold.

**Mitigation:**
- Hierarchical bootstrap CIs everywhere
- Abstention via the CI-width criterion
- Multiple seeds (5 final) and averaging
- Explicit noise-floor experiment: shuffle labels within regions and show $G_{ij}(r)$ becomes noise

### Risk 7: Compute cost

**Probability:** Medium. Pairwise MTL training across many task pairs, multiple seeds, multiple architectures is expensive.

**Mitigation:**
- 2-4 H100 GPUs per available tooling
- Reuse trained encoders where possible
- Pilot uses 3 seeds; final uses 5
- Limit final task-pair count to ~80-120 across all datasets
- Stage compute: don't run final architecture variants until pilot greenlights

### Risk 8: Phenomenon exists but is too dataset-specific

**Probability:** Medium-low. Phenomenon should generalize across molecular datasets, but might not generalize beyond chemistry.

**Mitigation:**
- Own the molecular scope explicitly. The paper is about sparse molecular MTL with chemical structure.
- Briefly speculate on generalizability in discussion without claiming it
- Future work section discusses extension to other domains

## Part 5: Phase 0 — Pilot

### 5.1 Goal

Decide whether the project is worth pursuing. Test the phenomenon claim and the predictor claim with minimal infrastructure.

### 5.2 Datasets

Tox21 (12 tasks, 100% overlap), SIDER (27 tasks, 100% overlap). High overlap removes the support-availability confound at this stage.

### 5.3 Task pairs

20-40 task pairs sampled to include:
- Within-mechanism pairs (e.g., NR vs. NR pathways in Tox21) — expected positive transfer
- Cross-mechanism pairs (NR vs. SR) — likely heterogeneous
- SOC-grouped vs. cross-SOC pairs in SIDER

### 5.4 Architecture

Reuse ICLR paper's GCN architecture: 3-layer GCN, hidden dim 256, global mean pooling, MLP heads. AdamW, lr 1e-3, batch 32, 30 epochs, early stopping on validation.

### 5.5 Region partitioning

Bemis-Murcko scaffolds. Cluster scaffolds into 3-5 groups using hierarchical clustering on Tanimoto similarity of scaffold fingerprints. Require minimum 50 compounds per region.

### 5.6 Experimental protocol

For each dataset:

1. Train STL models for each task. 3 seeds. Save checkpoints.
2. Train pairwise MTL models for each task pair. 3 seeds. Save checkpoints.
3. For each pair × region: compute $\Delta_{ij}(r)$, $\Delta_{i \leftarrow j}(r)$, $\Delta_{j \leftarrow i}(r)$ on test split with hierarchical-bootstrap 95% CI.
4. For each pair × region: compute $G_{ij}(r)$ from gradients on training compounds in region. Hierarchical-bootstrap CI on $G_{ij}(r)$.
5. For each pair: compute $S_{ij}$ (sign heterogeneity, pair and task-specific), $H_{ij}$ (heterogeneity index), $C_{ij}$ (cancellation).
6. Test predictor: across all valid regions, does $G_{ij}(r)$ predict $\text{sign}(\Delta_{ij}(r))$? Compute AUROC and Spearman.
7. Build random-partition null distributions (200 random partitions per dataset) and compare scaffold-based metrics against the null.
8. Filter task pairs by the four-criteria "meaningful pair" definition before computing prevalence.

### 5.7 Green-light criteria (BOTH required)

**Phenomenon check:**
- At least 5 task pairs across the two datasets show stable sign heterogeneity (passes $|\Delta| > 1.5$ AUC threshold AND CI test in at least one positive and one negative region)
- AND at least 15% of meaningful task pairs (per the four-criteria definition in Section 1.3) exhibit sign heterogeneity
- AND scaffold-based heterogeneity prevalence exceeds the 95th percentile of the random-partition null distribution

**Predictor check:**
- Local gradient affinity AUROC for regional sign prediction $\geq 0.70$
- AND local gradient affinity AUROC exceeds best non-gradient baseline (scaffold similarity, regional label correlation, embedding distance) by at least 0.07
- AND Spearman $\rho$ between $G_{ij}(r)$ and $\Delta_{ij}(r) \geq 0.45$
- AND results stable across 3 seeds (sign of $G_{ij}(r)$ agrees in $\geq 80\%$ of regions across seeds)

### 5.8 Decision tree

- **Both pass:** Full project proceed. NeurIPS target.
- **Phenomenon passes, predictor fails:** Workshop paper on phenomenon discovery; or invest in better predictors (TAG-style, Fisher-normalized) before deciding.
- **Phenomenon fails, predictor untestable:** Drop project or pivot to "conditions under which global affinity is sufficient" framing (much weaker, probably workshop only).
- **Both fail:** Drop project.

### 5.9 Pilot deliverables

- Sign heterogeneity prevalence table across datasets, with random-partition null comparison
- Pair-averaged AND task-specific regional benefit tables
- Predictor AUROC comparison vs. baselines
- One compelling task-pair example for Figure 2
- Hierarchical-bootstrap stability assessment
- Decision document with explicit go/no-go reasoning

## Part 8: Phase 3 — Utility Evaluation

This phase establishes practical value: using local affinity to make sharing decisions. Real architectures, real baselines, honest comparison.

### 8.1 Goal

Show that local-affinity-guided sharing reduces regional negative transfer while preserving regional positive transfer, and either matches or beats existing routing methods.

### 8.2 Sharing strategies compared

**Single-task baselines:**
1. STL: train each task independently

**Static all-task baselines:**
2. All-task MTL: train one shared encoder on all tasks
3. Pairwise MTL union: train pairwise MTL for each pair, ensemble at test

**Global grouping baselines:**
4. Random grouping (10 trials averaged)
5. Global gradient affinity grouping (your ICLR paper)
6. TAG-style affinity grouping (Fifty et al. or close re-implementation)

**Adaptive sharing baselines:**
7. MMoE: mixture-of-experts with task-specific gates
8. One additional adaptive sharing method: DynaShare if implementation is clean and faithful, otherwise Cross-Stitch Networks (Misra et al.) or Sluice Networks (Ruder et al.). Decision made before Phase 3 begins based on engineering assessment. Bad implementation is worse than no implementation; the baseline must be defensible.

**Local affinity strategies (the proposed approach):**
9. Local affinity hard sharing: For each region, pre-compute $G_{ij}(r)$ for all task pairs. Train region-conditional MTL where shared parameters are active only between task pairs with $G_{ij}(r) > \tau_+$ in that region.
10. Local affinity soft sharing: Use $G_{ij}(r)$ as a continuous gate weight on cross-task parameter sharing per region.
11. Local affinity with abstention: Like (9), but for low-support regions, default to STL prediction.

### 8.3 Implementation of local affinity sharing architecture

The proposed architecture must be implemented carefully. One reasonable approach:

- Shared encoder produces representation $\phi(x)$
- Region assignment function $r(x)$ assigns each compound to a region (scaffold-based, deterministic)
- Task heads receive $\phi(x)$ plus region identity
- For each task pair $(i, j)$ and region $r$, a learned binary gate $\gamma_{ij}(r)$ controls whether encoder gradients from task $i$ flow back through shared parameters when task $j$ is being trained on region $r$
- $\gamma_{ij}(r)$ is initialized using $G_{ij}(r)$ pre-computed from a short pretraining run, then optionally fine-tuned

This is a significant engineering effort. The paper includes a clean public implementation.

### 8.4 Datasets

- Tox21, SIDER, ToxCast (high overlap)
- TDC ADMET, kinase panel (sparse)

### 8.5 Metrics

**Performance metrics:**
- Average per-task AUC/RMSE (across tasks)
- Worst-region performance (per task, then averaged)
- Average regional performance
- Sample-weighted average performance

**Negative transfer metrics:**
- Negative transfer rate: fraction of (task, region) pairs where method underperforms STL by at least 1.5 AUC
- Severe negative transfer rate: underperforms by at least 3 AUC
- Average regional negative transfer magnitude
- Task-specific negative transfer rate (using $\Delta_{i \leftarrow j}$ rather than pair average)

**Decision-quality metrics:**
- Calibration of share/separate decisions: when method predicts "share is beneficial," how often is it correct?
- Reliability diagrams for share/separate predictions
- Expected calibration error (ECE) on share/separate decisions
- Held-out task-pair calibration: fit decision rule on training task pairs, evaluate calibration on held-out pairs
- AUROC of sharing decisions vs. ground-truth regional benefit

**Efficiency metrics:**
- Training compute (GPU-hours)
- Inference latency
- Number of effective parameters

### 8.6 Statistical comparison

Pairwise comparisons between methods using paired tests across (task, region) combinations. Bonferroni correction across the comparison family. Hierarchical-bootstrap CIs on all reported numbers.

### 8.7 Expected outcomes and pre-committed interpretation

- If local affinity sharing matches or beats DynaShare/MMoE on average performance AND has substantially lower negative transfer rate: strong result
- If local affinity sharing matches DynaShare/MMoE on average but is interpretable while they are not: moderate result, framed around interpretability and calibration
- If local affinity sharing substantially underperforms DynaShare/MMoE on average: still publishable as a diagnostic, but the contribution shifts

The paper must commit to honesty here. If routing methods beat local affinity on raw performance, the paper says so and reframes the contribution around regional diagnosis and calibrated abstention rather than claiming SOTA.

- Negative transfer rate comparison (key figure)
- Calibration analysis with reliability diagrams

## Part 10: Phase 5 — Theory and Discussion

### 10.1 Theoretical contributions

The paper does not claim a major theorem but includes:

- A formal definition of region-conditional task affinity
- A statement (with proof sketch in appendix) that under structured covariate shift across regions, the global gradient affinity is a function of (a) regional affinities, (b) regional sample weights, (c) the cosine geometry — and is not in general a weighted average of regional affinities
- A signal-to-noise analysis of when $G_{ij}(r)$ is a reliable estimator: depends on per-region sample size, gradient norm, and noise structure
- Conditions under which local-affinity-guided sharing is provably beneficial in expectation (under simplified assumptions)

These are framed as supporting analysis, not primary contributions. The paper is empirical.

### 10.2 Connection to ICLR paper

Explicit section showing the ICLR paper's overlap requirement is a special case. When tasks have full overlap (Tox21, SIDER), the global gradient affinity is reliable because all compounds are shared. When overlap is partial, regions of the input space have asymmetric task coverage, and global affinity becomes a function of which regions happen to be sampled. The NeurIPS paper generalizes the ICLR result by showing that even at full overlap, regional structure can make global affinity misleading. We generalize the principle from global sample overlap to regional support-validity.

### 10.3 When global affinity is sufficient

The paper explicitly identifies regimes where global affinity is fine:
- High-overlap datasets where all task pairs have positive (or all negative) transfer everywhere
- Task pairs where regional effects are roughly uniform
- Coarse-grained planning where regional decisions are not actionable

This honest framing strengthens the paper.

### 10.4 Limitations

- Molecular focus: extension to vision/NLP is future work
- Region partitioning is itself a modeling choice; the paper doesn't fully solve "what is the right region structure"
- Local-affinity sharing architecture has computational overhead vs. all-task MTL
- Regional sign prediction requires sufficient per-region samples; very sparse settings remain challenging
- Hierarchical bootstrap assumes scaffold groupings have approximately exchangeable substructure within clusters

## Part 12: Paper Structure

### 12.1 Abstract

Multi-task learning methods typically treat task compatibility as a global relationship between task pairs. We show this assumption can fail: the same pair of molecular prediction tasks can exhibit positive transfer in one chemical region and negative transfer in another. This *transfer sign heterogeneity* causes global task-affinity scores to cancel opposing regional effects and mislead task grouping. We define region-conditional transfer benefit, introduce support-valid regional gradient affinity as a diagnostic, and show that local gradient affinity predicts regional transfer beyond scaffold similarity and label-correlation baselines. Across five molecular datasets, sign heterogeneity is common (X% of meaningful task pairs, exceeding random-partition null at p<0.05) and produces measurable cancellation in global affinity. Local gradient affinity predicts regional transfer sign with AUROC Y, adding incremental $R^2$ of Z over chemistry baselines. Using local affinity for share/separate/abstain decisions reduces regional negative transfer by W% while matching adaptive sharing on average performance. Our results suggest task affinity should not be treated as a scalar relationship in domains where transfer is regionally heterogeneous.

### 12.2 Introduction (1.5 pages)

Lead with the phenomenon, not the method:

- MTL needs task compatibility decisions. Existing methods (TAG, DynaShare, MMoE) provide task-pair-level affinity scores or implicit instance-level routing.
- These methods share an unstated assumption: task compatibility is a property of the task pair. We show this assumption can fail.
- Concrete example: introduce one canonical sign-heterogeneous task pair from results, with the regional benefit figure inline.
- Generalization: across five datasets, this happens often enough that global task affinity is systematically misleading.
- The ICLR paper showed gradient affinity requires shared samples. We show that even with shared samples, the same pair can show opposite transfer in different regions, generalizing the principle from sample overlap to regional support-validity.
- Contribution stack:
  1. **Phenomenon**: define and measure transfer sign heterogeneity (pair-averaged and task-specific)
  2. **Failure mode**: characterize global affinity cancellation
  3. **Diagnostic**: region-conditional gradient affinity with explicit support-validity, evaluated against chemistry baselines
  4. **Utility**: local-affinity sharing reduces negative transfer
  5. **Robustness**: phenomenon holds across architectures, partitionings, and sample sizes
- Field-level implication: task compatibility is sign-changing, support-dependent, and region-conditional. Single-scalar task affinity is sufficient only when transfer is regionally stable.

### 13.4 Final claims

The paper claims, in order of strength:

1. Transfer sign heterogeneity exists and is common in molecular MTL, both pair-averaged and task-specific
2. Global affinity averages over regional effects and is misleading for sign-heterogeneous pairs (cancellation phenomenon)
3. Region-conditional gradient affinity, computed with explicit support-validity, predicts regional transfer with measurable incremental value over chemistry baselines
4. Local-affinity-guided sharing reduces negative transfer while maintaining average performance
5. The phenomenon is robust to partitioning, architecture, and sample size choices, and exceeds random-partition null distributions

The paper does not claim:
- Beating routing methods on average performance (unless it does)
- Generalization beyond molecules
- A complete theoretical characterization
- Novel sample-overlap requirement (generalized from ICLR)

## Part 14: Honest Bottom Line

The contribution is the claim stack, not the components. Individually, gradient affinity is not new, scaffolds are not new, regional analysis is not new, MTL is not new. The contribution is the field-level claim that task affinity should not be treated as a scalar in domains where transfer is regionally heterogeneous, supported by a phenomenon (sign heterogeneity), a failure mode (cancellation), a diagnostic (region-conditional gradient affinity), and a utility demonstration (local sharing decisions).

This framing is what makes the project a NeurIPS candidate rather than an incremental MTL method paper. It's also what makes scoop accusations harder: no prior paper has made this specific claim stack, even though the components are all in the literature.

The pilot is the gating decision. Conditional on the pilot passing both checks AND scaffold similarity not explaining everything, this is a 35-45% NeurIPS main candidate. The probability is what it is regardless of how well the contribution is framed; reviewers respond to evidence, not slogans. But framing determines whether reviewers grasp the contribution quickly enough to evaluate the evidence on its merits, so the phenomenon-first framing matters operationally even if it doesn't shift acceptance odds dramatically.

The single biggest scientific risk is that scaffold similarity explains everything and gradient affinity adds no incremental value. The incremental-$R^2$ test in Phase 2 is the deciding experiment for that question, and the paper's centerpiece result.

The single biggest execution risk is the utility experiment in Phase 3, because the local-affinity sharing architecture is a significant engineering effort and adaptive sharing baselines (MMoE plus DynaShare/Cross-Stitch/Sluice) require honest implementation.

If both risks are managed and the pilot greenlights, this is a real paper.

## Part 3: Risk Register and Mitigations

### Risk 1 (PRIMARY SCIENTIFIC RISK): Scaffold similarity explains everything

**Probability:** Medium-high. Two regions in the same scaffold cluster are by definition chemically similar; this might fully explain regional transfer patterns and reduce the gradient-affinity contribution to noise.

**Why this is the primary risk:** If true, the paper has no methodological contribution. The phenomenon would still exist, but the predictor's added value over a chemistry baseline would be zero, which would force a workshop paper.

**Detection:** In Phase 2, run joint regression $\Delta_{ij}(r) \sim \text{ScaffoldSim}_{ij}(r) + G_{ij}(r) + \text{LabelCorr}_{ij}(r)$ and report incremental $R^2$ and partial correlation of each predictor. This is a centerpiece result, not an aside.

**Mitigation:**
- Latent embedding partitioning as alternative (decouples chemical similarity from learned task structure)
- Within-scaffold-cluster analysis showing $G_{ij}(r)$ varies meaningfully even when scaffold structure is held constant
- Report incremental $R^2$ for $G_{ij}(r)$ over chemistry baselines as a primary result
- Consider that gradient affinity may capture *task-specific* representational alignment that scaffold similarity cannot, especially across diverse molecular families

**Kill criterion:** $G_{ij}(r)$ contributes less than $0.05$ incremental $R^2$ over scaffold similarity in joint regression, across multiple datasets. Reframe paper around scaffold-based regional structure rather than gradient affinity, and pivot to workshop submission.

### Risk 2: Sign heterogeneity is rare or absent

**Probability:** Medium. Plausible that MTL benefit is roughly monotonic across regions for most pairs.

**Detection:** Pilot Phase 0 directly measures prevalence.

**Mitigation:**
- Test deliberately weakly-related task pairs (cross-domain Tox21+ADME) where heterogeneity is more likely than for tightly-related pairs
- Test high-overlap datasets first to remove confounds
- Define meaningful effect sizes upfront (the 1.5 AUC threshold)
- Use sufficient region partitioning resolution (3-8 regions per dataset)
- Examine task-specific (asymmetric) heterogeneity as a separate channel — even pairs that look symmetric may show asymmetric regional transfer

**Kill criterion:** Fewer than 5 task pairs across all datasets show stable sign heterogeneity passing the CI test. Project becomes a workshop paper or is dropped.

### Risk 3: Local gradient affinity does not predict regional transfer sign

**Probability:** Medium-low. Your ICLR paper showed global cosine works above 30% overlap; locally restricted versions should also work given enough samples per region.

**Detection:** Phase 0 pilot measures regional sign prediction AUROC.

**Mitigation:**
- TAG-style affinity as backup metric (pre-registered as ablation)
- Last-shared-layer gradients vs. all-encoder gradients (tested as ablation)
- Fisher-normalized gradients (tested as ablation)
- Larger region sizes if small-region noise dominates

**Kill criterion:** Local gradient affinity AUROC for regional sign prediction does not exceed best non-gradient baseline (scaffold similarity, regional label correlation, embedding distance) by at least 0.07. Project drops to workshop paper.

### Risk 4: Reviewers see this as DynaShare/MMoE rebranded

**Probability:** Medium. The most likely reviewer attack.

**Mitigation:**
- Frame the contribution as diagnosis and measurement, not as a competing architecture
- Include MMoE and one other adaptive sharing baseline (Cross-Stitch, Sluice, or DynaShare if implementation is clean) as performance baselines
- Show calibrated regional sign prediction as something routing methods do not provide (their gating weights are not designed to predict regional MTL benefit)
- Demonstrate that local gradient affinity, computed before MTL training, predicts what routing methods learn during training. This positions local affinity as a *predictor* and *diagnostic*, not a competitor

### Risk 5: Region partitioning is itself a confound

**Probability:** Medium. Different partitionings might give different sign-heterogeneity prevalence.

**Mitigation:**
- Test all three partitioning schemes (scaffolds, latent clusters, kNN)
- Report agreement: phenomenon must hold across at least two
- Random-partition negative control with proper null distribution: scaffold-based prevalence must exceed 95th percentile of random-partition null
- Vary number of regions (3, 5, 8, 10) and report stability

### Risk 6: Small-region statistical noise

**Probability:** High. Some regions will have small samples even after the $n_{\min}$ threshold.

**Mitigation:**
- Hierarchical bootstrap CIs everywhere
- Abstention via the CI-width criterion
- Multiple seeds (5 final) and averaging
- Explicit noise-floor experiment: shuffle labels within regions and show $G_{ij}(r)$ becomes noise

### Risk 7: Compute cost

**Probability:** Medium. Pairwise MTL training across many task pairs, multiple seeds, multiple architectures is expensive.

**Mitigation:**
- 2-4 H100 GPUs per available tooling
- Reuse trained encoders where possible
- Pilot uses 3 seeds; final uses 5
- Limit final task-pair count to ~80-120 across all datasets
- Stage compute: don't run final architecture variants until pilot greenlights

### Risk 8: Phenomenon exists but is too dataset-specific

**Probability:** Medium-low. Phenomenon should generalize across molecular datasets, but might not generalize beyond chemistry.

**Mitigation:**
- Own the molecular scope explicitly. The paper is about sparse molecular MTL with chemical structure.
- Briefly speculate on generalizability in discussion without claiming it
- Future work section discusses extension to other domains

## Part 5: Phase 0 — Pilot

### 5.1 Goal

Decide whether the project is worth pursuing. Test the phenomenon claim and the predictor claim with minimal infrastructure.

### 5.2 Datasets

Tox21 (12 tasks, 100% overlap), SIDER (27 tasks, 100% overlap). High overlap removes the support-availability confound at this stage.

### 5.3 Task pairs

20-40 task pairs sampled to include:
- Within-mechanism pairs (e.g., NR vs. NR pathways in Tox21) — expected positive transfer
- Cross-mechanism pairs (NR vs. SR) — likely heterogeneous
- SOC-grouped vs. cross-SOC pairs in SIDER

### 5.4 Architecture

Reuse ICLR paper's GCN architecture: 3-layer GCN, hidden dim 256, global mean pooling, MLP heads. AdamW, lr 1e-3, batch 32, 30 epochs, early stopping on validation.

### 5.5 Region partitioning

Bemis-Murcko scaffolds. Cluster scaffolds into 3-5 groups using hierarchical clustering on Tanimoto similarity of scaffold fingerprints. Require minimum 50 compounds per region.

### 5.6 Experimental protocol

For each dataset:

1. Train STL models for each task. 3 seeds. Save checkpoints.
2. Train pairwise MTL models for each task pair. 3 seeds. Save checkpoints.
3. For each pair × region: compute $\Delta_{ij}(r)$, $\Delta_{i \leftarrow j}(r)$, $\Delta_{j \leftarrow i}(r)$ on test split with hierarchical-bootstrap 95% CI.
4. For each pair × region: compute $G_{ij}(r)$ from gradients on training compounds in region. Hierarchical-bootstrap CI on $G_{ij}(r)$.
5. For each pair: compute $S_{ij}$ (sign heterogeneity, pair and task-specific), $H_{ij}$ (heterogeneity index), $C_{ij}$ (cancellation).
6. Test predictor: across all valid regions, does $G_{ij}(r)$ predict $\text{sign}(\Delta_{ij}(r))$? Compute AUROC and Spearman.
7. Build random-partition null distributions (200 random partitions per dataset) and compare scaffold-based metrics against the null.
8. Filter task pairs by the four-criteria "meaningful pair" definition before computing prevalence.

### 5.7 Green-light criteria (BOTH required)

**Phenomenon check:**
- At least 5 task pairs across the two datasets show stable sign heterogeneity (passes $|\Delta| > 1.5$ AUC threshold AND CI test in at least one positive and one negative region)
- AND at least 15% of meaningful task pairs (per the four-criteria definition in Section 1.3) exhibit sign heterogeneity
- AND scaffold-based heterogeneity prevalence exceeds the 95th percentile of the random-partition null distribution

**Predictor check:**
- Local gradient affinity AUROC for regional sign prediction $\geq 0.70$
- AND local gradient affinity AUROC exceeds best non-gradient baseline (scaffold similarity, regional label correlation, embedding distance) by at least 0.07
- AND Spearman $\rho$ between $G_{ij}(r)$ and $\Delta_{ij}(r) \geq 0.45$
- AND results stable across 3 seeds (sign of $G_{ij}(r)$ agrees in $\geq 80\%$ of regions across seeds)

### 5.8 Decision tree

- **Both pass:** Full project proceed. NeurIPS target.
- **Phenomenon passes, predictor fails:** Workshop paper on phenomenon discovery; or invest in better predictors (TAG-style, Fisher-normalized) before deciding.
- **Phenomenon fails, predictor untestable:** Drop project or pivot to "conditions under which global affinity is sufficient" framing (much weaker, probably workshop only).
- **Both fail:** Drop project.

### 5.9 Pilot deliverables

- Sign heterogeneity prevalence table across datasets, with random-partition null comparison
- Pair-averaged AND task-specific regional benefit tables
- Predictor AUROC comparison vs. baselines
- One compelling task-pair example for Figure 2
- Hierarchical-bootstrap stability assessment
- Decision document with explicit go/no-go reasoning

## Part 7: Phase 2 — Predictive Evaluation

### 7.1 Goal

Show that local gradient affinity predicts regional transfer better than alternatives, and crucially that it adds value beyond chemistry baselines.

### 7.2 Prediction targets

For each (task pair, region) with valid local support:
- Binary: $\text{sign}(\Delta_{ij}(r))$
- Continuous: $\Delta_{ij}(r)$ value
- Three-class: positive transfer / negative transfer / no significant transfer
- Task-specific equivalents: $\text{sign}(\Delta_{i \leftarrow j}(r))$ and $\text{sign}(\Delta_{j \leftarrow i}(r))$

### 7.3 Predictor methods

**Primary methods:**
1. Local gradient cosine: $G_{ij}(r)$
2. Local gradient cosine + local support size as features (small classifier)
3. TAG-style local affinity: effect of one task's regional gradient step on the other task's regional loss

**Non-gradient baselines:**
4. Scaffold similarity: mean Tanimoto similarity between $D_i(r)$ and $D_j(r)$ scaffolds
5. Regional embedding distance: $\|\text{mean}(\phi(D_i(r))) - \text{mean}(\phi(D_j(r)))\|$
6. Regional label correlation: Pearson correlation of labels for compounds with both labels in $r$ (when available)
7. Regional MMD between task input distributions
8. Global gradient affinity: $G_{ij}^{\text{global}}$ from your ICLR paper, applied to all regions of a pair (constant predictor)
9. Regional Task2Vec embedding similarity (if computationally feasible)

**Joint predictor (centerpiece for primary risk):**
10. Logistic regression: $\text{sign}(\Delta_{ij}(r)) \sim G_{ij}(r) + \text{ScaffoldSim} + \text{LabelCorr} + \text{EmbeddingDist}$ — used to compute incremental contribution of $G_{ij}(r)$ over chemistry baselines

### 7.4 Metrics

- AUROC for sign prediction (pair-averaged AND task-specific)
- AUPRC
- Balanced accuracy
- Spearman $\rho$ with $\Delta_{ij}(r)$
- Calibration (reliability diagram, ECE)
- Hierarchical-bootstrap CIs on all metrics
- **Incremental $R^2$ of $G_{ij}(r)$ in joint regression — primary scientific result**

## Part 8: Phase 3 — Utility Evaluation

This phase establishes practical value: using local affinity to make sharing decisions. Real architectures, real baselines, honest comparison.

### 8.1 Goal

Show that local-affinity-guided sharing reduces regional negative transfer while preserving regional positive transfer, and either matches or beats existing routing methods.

### 8.2 Sharing strategies compared

**Single-task baselines:**
1. STL: train each task independently

**Static all-task baselines:**
2. All-task MTL: train one shared encoder on all tasks
3. Pairwise MTL union: train pairwise MTL for each pair, ensemble at test

**Global grouping baselines:**
4. Random grouping (10 trials averaged)
5. Global gradient affinity grouping (your ICLR paper)
6. TAG-style affinity grouping (Fifty et al. or close re-implementation)

**Adaptive sharing baselines:**
7. MMoE: mixture-of-experts with task-specific gates
8. One additional adaptive sharing method: DynaShare if implementation is clean and faithful, otherwise Cross-Stitch Networks (Misra et al.) or Sluice Networks (Ruder et al.). Decision made before Phase 3 begins based on engineering assessment. Bad implementation is worse than no implementation; the baseline must be defensible.

**Local affinity strategies (the proposed approach):**
9. Local affinity hard sharing: For each region, pre-compute $G_{ij}(r)$ for all task pairs. Train region-conditional MTL where shared parameters are active only between task pairs with $G_{ij}(r) > \tau_+$ in that region.
10. Local affinity soft sharing: Use $G_{ij}(r)$ as a continuous gate weight on cross-task parameter sharing per region.
11. Local affinity with abstention: Like (9), but for low-support regions, default to STL prediction.

### 8.3 Implementation of local affinity sharing architecture

The proposed architecture must be implemented carefully. One reasonable approach:

- Shared encoder produces representation $\phi(x)$
- Region assignment function $r(x)$ assigns each compound to a region (scaffold-based, deterministic)
- Task heads receive $\phi(x)$ plus region identity
- For each task pair $(i, j)$ and region $r$, a learned binary gate $\gamma_{ij}(r)$ controls whether encoder gradients from task $i$ flow back through shared parameters when task $j$ is being trained on region $r$
- $\gamma_{ij}(r)$ is initialized using $G_{ij}(r)$ pre-computed from a short pretraining run, then optionally fine-tuned

This is a significant engineering effort. The paper includes a clean public implementation.

### 8.4 Datasets

- Tox21, SIDER, ToxCast (high overlap)
- TDC ADMET, kinase panel (sparse)

### 8.5 Metrics

**Performance metrics:**
- Average per-task AUC/RMSE (across tasks)
- Worst-region performance (per task, then averaged)
- Average regional performance
- Sample-weighted average performance

**Negative transfer metrics:**
- Negative transfer rate: fraction of (task, region) pairs where method underperforms STL by at least 1.5 AUC
- Severe negative transfer rate: underperforms by at least 3 AUC
- Average regional negative transfer magnitude
- Task-specific negative transfer rate (using $\Delta_{i \leftarrow j}$ rather than pair average)

**Decision-quality metrics:**
- Calibration of share/separate decisions: when method predicts "share is beneficial," how often is it correct?
- Reliability diagrams for share/separate predictions
- Expected calibration error (ECE) on share/separate decisions
- Held-out task-pair calibration: fit decision rule on training task pairs, evaluate calibration on held-out pairs
- AUROC of sharing decisions vs. ground-truth regional benefit

**Efficiency metrics:**
- Training compute (GPU-hours)
- Inference latency
- Number of effective parameters

### 8.6 Statistical comparison

Pairwise comparisons between methods using paired tests across (task, region) combinations. Bonferroni correction across the comparison family. Hierarchical-bootstrap CIs on all reported numbers.

### 8.7 Expected outcomes and pre-committed interpretation

- If local affinity sharing matches or beats DynaShare/MMoE on average performance AND has substantially lower negative transfer rate: strong result
- If local affinity sharing matches DynaShare/MMoE on average but is interpretable while they are not: moderate result, framed around interpretability and calibration
- If local affinity sharing substantially underperforms DynaShare/MMoE on average: still publishable as a diagnostic, but the contribution shifts

The paper must commit to honesty here. If routing methods beat local affinity on raw performance, the paper says so and reframes the contribution around regional diagnosis and calibrated abstention rather than claiming SOTA.

### 8.8 Phase 3 deliverables

- Main performance table across all methods and datasets

## Part 10: Phase 5 — Theory and Discussion

### 10.1 Theoretical contributions

The paper does not claim a major theorem but includes:

- A formal definition of region-conditional task affinity
- A statement (with proof sketch in appendix) that under structured covariate shift across regions, the global gradient affinity is a function of (a) regional affinities, (b) regional sample weights, (c) the cosine geometry — and is not in general a weighted average of regional affinities
- A signal-to-noise analysis of when $G_{ij}(r)$ is a reliable estimator: depends on per-region sample size, gradient norm, and noise structure
- Conditions under which local-affinity-guided sharing is provably beneficial in expectation (under simplified assumptions)

These are framed as supporting analysis, not primary contributions. The paper is empirical.

### 10.2 Connection to ICLR paper

Explicit section showing the ICLR paper's overlap requirement is a special case. When tasks have full overlap (Tox21, SIDER), the global gradient affinity is reliable because all compounds are shared. When overlap is partial, regions of the input space have asymmetric task coverage, and global affinity becomes a function of which regions happen to be sampled. The NeurIPS paper generalizes the ICLR result by showing that even at full overlap, regional structure can make global affinity misleading. We generalize the principle from global sample overlap to regional support-validity.

### 10.3 When global affinity is sufficient

The paper explicitly identifies regimes where global affinity is fine:
- High-overlap datasets where all task pairs have positive (or all negative) transfer everywhere
- Task pairs where regional effects are roughly uniform
- Coarse-grained planning where regional decisions are not actionable

This honest framing strengthens the paper.

### 10.4 Limitations

- Molecular focus: extension to vision/NLP is future work
- Region partitioning is itself a modeling choice; the paper doesn't fully solve "what is the right region structure"
- Local-affinity sharing architecture has computational overhead vs. all-task MTL
- Regional sign prediction requires sufficient per-region samples; very sparse settings remain challenging
- Hierarchical bootstrap assumes scaffold groupings have approximately exchangeable substructure within clusters

## Part 12: Paper Structure

### 12.1 Abstract

Multi-task learning methods typically treat task compatibility as a global relationship between task pairs. We show this assumption can fail: the same pair of molecular prediction tasks can exhibit positive transfer in one chemical region and negative transfer in another. This *transfer sign heterogeneity* causes global task-affinity scores to cancel opposing regional effects and mislead task grouping. We define region-conditional transfer benefit, introduce support-valid regional gradient affinity as a diagnostic, and show that local gradient affinity predicts regional transfer beyond scaffold similarity and label-correlation baselines. Across five molecular datasets, sign heterogeneity is common (X% of meaningful task pairs, exceeding random-partition null at p<0.05) and produces measurable cancellation in global affinity. Local gradient affinity predicts regional transfer sign with AUROC Y, adding incremental $R^2$ of Z over chemistry baselines. Using local affinity for share/separate/abstain decisions reduces regional negative transfer by W% while matching adaptive sharing on average performance. Our results suggest task affinity should not be treated as a scalar relationship in domains where transfer is regionally heterogeneous.

### 12.2 Introduction (1.5 pages)

Lead with the phenomenon, not the method:

- MTL needs task compatibility decisions. Existing methods (TAG, DynaShare, MMoE) provide task-pair-level affinity scores or implicit instance-level routing.
- These methods share an unstated assumption: task compatibility is a property of the task pair. We show this assumption can fail.
- Concrete example: introduce one canonical sign-heterogeneous task pair from results, with the regional benefit figure inline.
- Generalization: across five datasets, this happens often enough that global task affinity is systematically misleading.
- The ICLR paper showed gradient affinity requires shared samples. We show that even with shared samples, the same pair can show opposite transfer in different regions, generalizing the principle from sample overlap to regional support-validity.
- Contribution stack:
  1. **Phenomenon**: define and measure transfer sign heterogeneity (pair-averaged and task-specific)
  2. **Failure mode**: characterize global affinity cancellation
  3. **Diagnostic**: region-conditional gradient affinity with explicit support-validity, evaluated against chemistry baselines
  4. **Utility**: local-affinity sharing reduces negative transfer
  5. **Robustness**: phenomenon holds across architectures, partitionings, and sample sizes
- Field-level implication: task compatibility is sign-changing, support-dependent, and region-conditional. Single-scalar task affinity is sufficient only when transfer is regionally stable.

## Part 14: Honest Bottom Line

The contribution is the claim stack, not the components. Individually, gradient affinity is not new, scaffolds are not new, regional analysis is not new, MTL is not new. The contribution is the field-level claim that task affinity should not be treated as a scalar in domains where transfer is regionally heterogeneous, supported by a phenomenon (sign heterogeneity), a failure mode (cancellation), a diagnostic (region-conditional gradient affinity), and a utility demonstration (local sharing decisions).

This framing is what makes the project a NeurIPS candidate rather than an incremental MTL method paper. It's also what makes scoop accusations harder: no prior paper has made this specific claim stack, even though the components are all in the literature.

The pilot is the gating decision. Conditional on the pilot passing both checks AND scaffold similarity not explaining everything, this is a 35-45% NeurIPS main candidate. The single biggest execution risk is the utility experiment in Phase 3, because the local-affinity sharing architecture is a significant engineering effort and adaptive sharing baselines (MMoE plus DynaShare/Cross-Stitch/Sluice) require honest implementation.

If both risks are managed and the pilot greenlights, this is a real paper.

## Part 3: Risk Register and Mitigations

### Risk 1 (PRIMARY SCIENTIFIC RISK): Scaffold similarity explains everything

**Probability:** Medium-high. Two regions in the same scaffold cluster are by definition chemically similar; this might fully explain regional transfer patterns and reduce the gradient-affinity contribution to noise.

**Why this is the primary risk:** If true, the paper has no methodological contribution. The phenomenon would still exist, but the predictor's added value over a chemistry baseline would be zero, which would force a workshop paper.

**Detection:** In Phase 2, run joint regression $\Delta_{ij}(r) \sim \text{ScaffoldSim}_{ij}(r) + G_{ij}(r) + \text{LabelCorr}_{ij}(r)$ and report incremental $R^2$ and partial correlation of each predictor. This is a centerpiece result, not an aside.

**Mitigation:**
- Latent embedding partitioning as alternative (decouples chemical similarity from learned task structure)
- Within-scaffold-cluster analysis showing $G_{ij}(r)$ varies meaningfully even when scaffold structure is held constant
- Report incremental $R^2$ for $G_{ij}(r)$ over chemistry baselines as a primary result
- Consider that gradient affinity may capture *task-specific* representational alignment that scaffold similarity cannot, especially across diverse molecular families

**Kill criterion:** $G_{ij}(r)$ contributes less than $0.05$ incremental $R^2$ over scaffold similarity in joint regression, across multiple datasets. Reframe paper around scaffold-based regional structure rather than gradient affinity, and pivot to workshop submission.

### Risk 2: Sign heterogeneity is rare or absent

**Probability:** Medium. Plausible that MTL benefit is roughly monotonic across regions for most pairs.

**Detection:** Pilot Phase 0 directly measures prevalence.

**Mitigation:**
- Test deliberately weakly-related task pairs (cross-domain Tox21+ADME) where heterogeneity is more likely than for tightly-related pairs
- Test high-overlap datasets first to remove confounds
- Define meaningful effect sizes upfront (the 1.5 AUC threshold)
- Use sufficient region partitioning resolution (3-8 regions per dataset)
- Examine task-specific (asymmetric) heterogeneity as a separate channel — even pairs that look symmetric may show asymmetric regional transfer

**Kill criterion:** Fewer than 5 task pairs across all datasets show stable sign heterogeneity passing the CI test. Project becomes a workshop paper or is dropped.

### Risk 3: Local gradient affinity does not predict regional transfer sign

**Probability:** Medium-low. Your ICLR paper showed global cosine works above 30% overlap; locally restricted versions should also work given enough samples per region.

**Detection:** Phase 0 pilot measures regional sign prediction AUROC.

**Mitigation:**
- TAG-style affinity as backup metric (pre-registered as ablation)
- Last-shared-layer gradients vs. all-encoder gradients (tested as ablation)
- Fisher-normalized gradients (tested as ablation)
- Larger region sizes if small-region noise dominates

**Kill criterion:** Local gradient affinity AUROC for regional sign prediction does not exceed best non-gradient baseline (scaffold similarity, regional label correlation, embedding distance) by at least 0.07. Project drops to workshop paper.

### Risk 4: Reviewers see this as DynaShare/MMoE rebranded

**Probability:** Medium. The most likely reviewer attack.

**Mitigation:**
- Frame the contribution as diagnosis and measurement, not as a competing architecture
- Include MMoE and one other adaptive sharing baseline (Cross-Stitch, Sluice, or DynaShare if implementation is clean) as performance baselines
- Show calibrated regional sign prediction as something routing methods do not provide (their gating weights are not designed to predict regional MTL benefit)
- Demonstrate that local gradient affinity, computed before MTL training, predicts what routing methods learn during training. This positions local affinity as a *predictor* and *diagnostic*, not a competitor

### Risk 5: Region partitioning is itself a confound

**Probability:** Medium. Different partitionings might give different sign-heterogeneity prevalence.

**Mitigation:**
- Test all three partitioning schemes (scaffolds, latent clusters, kNN)
- Report agreement: phenomenon must hold across at least two
- Random-partition negative control with proper null distribution: scaffold-based prevalence must exceed 95th percentile of random-partition null
- Vary number of regions (3, 5, 8, 10) and report stability

### Risk 6: Small-region statistical noise

**Probability:** High. Some regions will have small samples even after the $n_{\min}$ threshold.

**Mitigation:**
- Hierarchical bootstrap CIs everywhere
- Abstention via the CI-width criterion
- Multiple seeds (5 final) and averaging
- Explicit noise-floor experiment: shuffle labels within regions and show $G_{ij}(r)$ becomes noise

### Risk 7: Compute cost

**Probability:** Medium. Pairwise MTL training across many task pairs, multiple seeds, multiple architectures is expensive.

**Mitigation:**
- 2-4 H100 GPUs per available tooling
- Reuse trained encoders where possible
- Pilot uses 3 seeds; final uses 5
- Limit final task-pair count to ~80-120 across all datasets
- Stage compute: don't run final architecture variants until pilot greenlights

### Risk 8: Phenomenon exists but is too dataset-specific

**Probability:** Medium-low. Phenomenon should generalize across molecular datasets, but might not generalize beyond chemistry.

**Mitigation:**
- Own the molecular scope explicitly. The paper is about sparse molecular MTL with chemical structure.
- Briefly speculate on generalizability in discussion without claiming it
- Future work section discusses extension to other domains

## Part 5: Phase 0 — Pilot

### 5.1 Goal

Decide whether the project is worth pursuing. Test the phenomenon claim and the predictor claim with minimal infrastructure.

### 5.2 Datasets

Tox21 (12 tasks, 100% overlap), SIDER (27 tasks, 100% overlap). High overlap removes the support-availability confound at this stage.

### 5.3 Task pairs

20-40 task pairs sampled to include:
- Within-mechanism pairs (e.g., NR vs. NR pathways in Tox21) — expected positive transfer
- Cross-mechanism pairs (NR vs. SR) — likely heterogeneous
- SOC-grouped vs. cross-SOC pairs in SIDER

### 5.4 Architecture

Reuse ICLR paper's GCN architecture: 3-layer GCN, hidden dim 256, global mean pooling, MLP heads. AdamW, lr 1e-3, batch 32, 30 epochs, early stopping on validation.

### 5.5 Region partitioning

Bemis-Murcko scaffolds. Cluster scaffolds into 3-5 groups using hierarchical clustering on Tanimoto similarity of scaffold fingerprints. Require minimum 50 compounds per region.

### 5.6 Experimental protocol

For each dataset:

1. Train STL models for each task. 3 seeds. Save checkpoints.
2. Train pairwise MTL models for each task pair. 3 seeds. Save checkpoints.
3. For each pair × region: compute $\Delta_{ij}(r)$, $\Delta_{i \leftarrow j}(r)$, $\Delta_{j \leftarrow i}(r)$ on test split with hierarchical-bootstrap 95% CI.
4. For each pair × region: compute $G_{ij}(r)$ from gradients on training compounds in region. Hierarchical-bootstrap CI on $G_{ij}(r)$.
5. For each pair: compute $S_{ij}$ (sign heterogeneity, pair and task-specific), $H_{ij}$ (heterogeneity index), $C_{ij}$ (cancellation).
6. Test predictor: across all valid regions, does $G_{ij}(r)$ predict $\text{sign}(\Delta_{ij}(r))$? Compute AUROC and Spearman.
7. Build random-partition null distributions (200 random partitions per dataset) and compare scaffold-based metrics against the null.
8. Filter task pairs by the four-criteria "meaningful pair" definition before computing prevalence.

### 5.7 Green-light criteria (BOTH required)

**Phenomenon check:**
- At least 5 task pairs across the two datasets show stable sign heterogeneity (passes $|\Delta| > 1.5$ AUC threshold AND CI test in at least one positive and one negative region)
- AND at least 15% of meaningful task pairs (per the four-criteria definition in Section 1.3) exhibit sign heterogeneity
- AND scaffold-based heterogeneity prevalence exceeds the 95th percentile of the random-partition null distribution

**Predictor check:**
- Local gradient affinity AUROC for regional sign prediction $\geq 0.70$
- AND local gradient affinity AUROC exceeds best non-gradient baseline (scaffold similarity, regional label correlation, embedding distance) by at least 0.07
- AND Spearman $\rho$ between $G_{ij}(r)$ and $\Delta_{ij}(r) \geq 0.45$
- AND results stable across 3 seeds (sign of $G_{ij}(r)$ agrees in $\geq 80\%$ of regions across seeds)

### 5.8 Decision tree

- **Both pass:** Full project proceed. NeurIPS target.
- **Phenomenon passes, predictor fails:** Workshop paper on phenomenon discovery; or invest in better predictors (TAG-style, Fisher-normalized) before deciding.
- **Phenomenon fails, predictor untestable:** Drop project or pivot to "conditions under which global affinity is sufficient" framing (much weaker, probably workshop only).
- **Both fail:** Drop project.

### 5.9 Pilot deliverables

- Sign heterogeneity prevalence table across datasets, with random-partition null comparison
- Pair-averaged AND task-specific regional benefit tables
- Predictor AUROC comparison vs. baselines
- One compelling task-pair example for Figure 2
- Hierarchical-bootstrap stability assessment
- Decision document with explicit go/no-go reasoning

## Part 8: Phase 3 — Utility Evaluation

This phase establishes practical value: using local affinity to make sharing decisions. Real architectures, real baselines, honest comparison.

### 8.1 Goal

Show that local-affinity-guided sharing reduces regional negative transfer while preserving regional positive transfer, and either matches or beats existing routing methods.

### 8.2 Sharing strategies compared

**Single-task baselines:**
1. STL: train each task independently

**Static all-task baselines:**
2. All-task MTL: train one shared encoder on all tasks
3. Pairwise MTL union: train pairwise MTL for each pair, ensemble at test

**Global grouping baselines:**
4. Random grouping (10 trials averaged)
5. Global gradient affinity grouping (your ICLR paper)
6. TAG-style affinity grouping (Fifty et al. or close re-implementation)

**Adaptive sharing baselines:**
7. MMoE: mixture-of-experts with task-specific gates
8. One additional adaptive sharing method: DynaShare if implementation is clean and faithful, otherwise Cross-Stitch Networks (Misra et al.) or Sluice Networks (Ruder et al.). Decision made before Phase 3 begins based on engineering assessment. Bad implementation is worse than no implementation; the baseline must be defensible.

**Local affinity strategies (the proposed approach):**
9. Local affinity hard sharing: For each region, pre-compute $G_{ij}(r)$ for all task pairs. Train region-conditional MTL where shared parameters are active only between task pairs with $G_{ij}(r) > \tau_+$ in that region.
10. Local affinity soft sharing: Use $G_{ij}(r)$ as a continuous gate weight on cross-task parameter sharing per region.
11. Local affinity with abstention: Like (9), but for low-support regions, default to STL prediction.

### 8.3 Implementation of local affinity sharing architecture

The proposed architecture must be implemented carefully. One reasonable approach:

- Shared encoder produces representation $\phi(x)$
- Region assignment function $r(x)$ assigns each compound to a region (scaffold-based, deterministic)
- Task heads receive $\phi(x)$ plus region identity
- For each task pair $(i, j)$ and region $r$, a learned binary gate $\gamma_{ij}(r)$ controls whether encoder gradients from task $i$ flow back through shared parameters when task $j$ is being trained on region $r$
- $\gamma_{ij}(r)$ is initialized using $G_{ij}(r)$ pre-computed from a short pretraining run, then optionally fine-tuned

This is a significant engineering effort. The paper includes a clean public implementation.

### 8.4 Datasets

- Tox21, SIDER, ToxCast (high overlap)
- TDC ADMET, kinase panel (sparse)

### 8.5 Metrics

**Performance metrics:**
- Average per-task AUC/RMSE (across tasks)
- Worst-region performance (per task, then averaged)
- Average regional performance
- Sample-weighted average performance

**Negative transfer metrics:**
- Negative transfer rate: fraction of (task, region) pairs where method underperforms STL by at least 1.5 AUC
- Severe negative transfer rate: underperforms by at least 3 AUC
- Average regional negative transfer magnitude
- Task-specific negative transfer rate (using $\Delta_{i \leftarrow j}$ rather than pair average)

**Decision-quality metrics:**
- Calibration of share/separate decisions: when method predicts "share is beneficial," how often is it correct?
- Reliability diagrams for share/separate predictions
- Expected calibration error (ECE) on share/separate decisions
- Held-out task-pair calibration: fit decision rule on training task pairs, evaluate calibration on held-out pairs
- AUROC of sharing decisions vs. ground-truth regional benefit

**Efficiency metrics:**
- Training compute (GPU-hours)
- Inference latency
- Number of effective parameters

### 8.6 Statistical comparison

Pairwise comparisons between methods using paired tests across (task, region) combinations. Bonferroni correction across the comparison family. Hierarchical-bootstrap CIs on all reported numbers.

### 8.7 Expected outcomes and pre-committed interpretation

- If local affinity sharing matches or beats DynaShare/MMoE on average performance AND has substantially lower negative transfer rate: strong result
- If local affinity sharing matches DynaShare/MMoE on average but is interpretable while they are not: moderate result, framed around interpretability and calibration
- If local affinity sharing substantially underperforms DynaShare/MMoE on average: still publishable as a diagnostic, but the contribution shifts

The paper must commit to honesty here. If routing methods beat local affinity on raw performance, the paper says so and reframes the contribution around regional diagnosis and calibrated abstention rather than claiming SOTA.

## Part 10: Phase 5 — Theory and Discussion

### 10.1 Theoretical contributions

The paper does not claim a major theorem but includes:

- A formal definition of region-conditional task affinity
- A statement (with proof sketch in appendix) that under structured covariate shift across regions, the global gradient affinity is a function of (a) regional affinities, (b) regional sample weights, (c) the cosine geometry — and is not in general a weighted average of regional affinities
- A signal-to-noise analysis of when $G_{ij}(r)$ is a reliable estimator: depends on per-region sample size, gradient norm, and noise structure
- Conditions under which local-affinity-guided sharing is provably beneficial in expectation (under simplified assumptions)

These are framed as supporting analysis, not primary contributions. The paper is empirical.

### 10.2 Connection to ICLR paper

Explicit section showing the ICLR paper's overlap requirement is a special case. When tasks have full overlap (Tox21, SIDER), the global gradient affinity is reliable because all compounds are shared. When overlap is partial, regions of the input space have asymmetric task coverage, and global affinity becomes a function of which regions happen to be sampled. The NeurIPS paper generalizes the ICLR result by showing that even at full overlap, regional structure can make global affinity misleading. We generalize the principle from global sample overlap to regional support-validity.

### 10.3 When global affinity is sufficient

The paper explicitly identifies regimes where global affinity is fine:
- High-overlap datasets where all task pairs have positive (or all negative) transfer everywhere
- Task pairs where regional effects are roughly uniform
- Coarse-grained planning where regional decisions are not actionable

This honest framing strengthens the paper.

### 10.4 Limitations

- Molecular focus: extension to vision/NLP is future work
- Region partitioning is itself a modeling choice; the paper doesn't fully solve "what is the right region structure"
- Local-affinity sharing architecture has computational overhead vs. all-task MTL
- Regional sign prediction requires sufficient per-region samples; very sparse settings remain challenging
- Hierarchical bootstrap assumes scaffold groupings have approximately exchangeable substructure within clusters

## Part 12: Paper Structure

### 12.1 Abstract

Multi-task learning methods typically treat task compatibility as a global relationship between task pairs. We show this assumption can fail: the same pair of molecular prediction tasks can exhibit positive transfer in one chemical region and negative transfer in another. This *transfer sign heterogeneity* causes global task-affinity scores to cancel opposing regional effects and mislead task grouping. We define region-conditional transfer benefit, introduce support-valid regional gradient affinity as a diagnostic, and show that local gradient affinity predicts regional transfer beyond scaffold similarity and label-correlation baselines. Across five molecular datasets, sign heterogeneity is common (X% of meaningful task pairs, exceeding random-partition null at p<0.05) and produces measurable cancellation in global affinity. Local gradient affinity predicts regional transfer sign with AUROC Y, adding incremental $R^2$ of Z over chemistry baselines. Using local affinity for share/separate/abstain decisions reduces regional negative transfer by W% while matching adaptive sharing on average performance. Our results suggest task affinity should not be treated as a scalar relationship in domains where transfer is regionally heterogeneous.

### 12.2 Introduction (1.5 pages)

Lead with the phenomenon, not the method:

- MTL needs task compatibility decisions. Existing methods (TAG, DynaShare, MMoE) provide task-pair-level affinity scores or implicit instance-level routing.
- These methods share an unstated assumption: task compatibility is a property of the task pair. We show this assumption can fail.
- Concrete example: introduce one canonical sign-heterogeneous task pair from results, with the regional benefit figure inline.
- Generalization: across five datasets, this happens often enough that global task affinity is systematically misleading.
- The ICLR paper showed gradient affinity requires shared samples. We show that even with shared samples, the same pair can show opposite transfer in different regions, generalizing the principle from sample overlap to regional support-validity.
- Contribution stack:
  1. **Phenomenon**: define and measure transfer sign heterogeneity (pair-averaged and task-specific)
  2. **Failure mode**: characterize global affinity cancellation
  3. **Diagnostic**: region-conditional gradient affinity with explicit support-validity, evaluated against chemistry baselines
  4. **Utility**: local-affinity sharing reduces negative transfer
  5. **Robustness**: phenomenon holds across architectures, partitionings, and sample sizes
- Field-level implication: task compatibility is sign-changing, support-dependent, and region-conditional. Single-scalar task affinity is sufficient only when transfer is regionally stable.

## Part 14: Honest Bottom Line

The contribution is the claim stack, not the components. Individually, gradient affinity is not new, scaffolds are not new, regional analysis is not new, MTL is not new. The contribution is the field-level claim that task affinity should not be treated as a scalar in domains where transfer is regionally heterogeneous, supported by a phenomenon (sign heterogeneity), a failure mode (cancellation), a diagnostic (region-conditional gradient affinity), and a utility demonstration (local sharing decisions).

This framing is what makes the project a NeurIPS candidate rather than an incremental MTL method paper. It's also what makes scoop accusations harder: no prior paper has made this specific claim stack, even though the components are all in the literature.

The pilot is the gating decision. Conditional on the pilot passing both checks AND scaffold similarity not explaining everything, this is a 35-45% NeurIPS main candidate. The probability is what it is regardless of how well the contribution is framed; reviewers respond to evidence, not slogans. But framing determines whether reviewers grasp the contribution quickly enough to evaluate the evidence on its merits, so the phenomenon-first framing matters operationally even if it doesn't shift acceptance odds dramatically.

The single biggest scientific risk is that scaffold similarity explains everything and gradient affinity adds no incremental value. The incremental-$R^2$ test in Phase 2 is the deciding experiment for that question, and the paper's centerpiece result.

The single biggest execution risk is the utility experiment in Phase 3, because the local-affinity sharing architecture is a significant engineering effort and adaptive sharing baselines (MMoE plus DynaShare/Cross-Stitch/Sluice) require honest implementation.

If both risks are managed and the pilot greenlights, this is a real paper.

### 2.2 Data preprocessing pipeline

For each dataset, the preprocessing pipeline is:

**Step 1: Load raw data.** Read SMILES + label columns. Drop rows where SMILES fails RDKit parsing.

**Step 2: Standardize molecules.** Use RDKit's `MolStandardize.rdMolStandardize.Cleanup` followed by `LargestFragmentChooser` to handle salts and mixtures. Canonicalize SMILES.

**Step 3: Compute Bemis-Murcko scaffolds.** Use `rdkit.Chem.Scaffolds.MurckoScaffold.MurckoScaffoldSmiles(smiles, includeChirality=False)`. Empty scaffolds (acyclic molecules) are bucketed under a single "empty" scaffold. Record scaffold for each molecule.

**Step 4: Compute scaffold fingerprints.** For each unique scaffold, compute Morgan fingerprint (radius=2, 2048 bits) using `rdkit.Chem.AllChem.GetMorganFingerprintAsBitVect`. These are used for scaffold clustering in Section 2.6.1.

**Step 5: Compute molecular features.** Atom features (74-dim per atom) and bond features (12-dim per bond) following the standard molecular GNN featurization. Cache featurized graphs to disk.

**Step 6: Train/val/test split.** Use scaffold-stratified random split: 70% train, 15% val, 15% test. Splitting algorithm:
1. Group molecules by scaffold.
2. Sort scaffold groups by size, descending.
3. Greedily assign whole scaffold groups to train/val/test bins targeting the 70/15/15 ratio.
4. For Tox21/SIDER/ToxCast (full overlap), the same split is used for all tasks.
5. For sparse datasets, splits are computed per-task but use a shared global scaffold→bin assignment to ensure no scaffold leakage across tasks.

Random seed for splitting: 42. This split is fixed for the entire project; experimental seeds (3 for pilot, 5 for final) only vary model initialization and stochastic training, not data splits.

**Step 7: Persist split.** Save split assignments to `splits/{dataset}/split.parquet` with columns `[smiles, scaffold, split, task_labels...]`. All experiments load from this file.

### 2.3 Encoder architecture (primary)

The primary architecture is a Graph Convolutional Network. Implementation reuses the ICLR paper's encoder for continuity.

**Specification:**
- 3 graph convolution layers (GCNConv from PyTorch Geometric)
- Hidden dimension: 256
- Activation: ReLU after each conv
- Dropout: 0.1 between layers
- Global pooling: global mean pool
- Output: $d = 256$-dimensional graph embedding

**Task heads:**
- Per-task MLP: $[256, 128, 1]$
- ReLU between hidden layers, no activation on output (logits for classification, raw values for regression)
- Dropout 0.1 in the hidden layer

**Total parameters (Tox21, 12 tasks):** approximately 500K

### 2.4 STL training protocol

For each task $i$ and seed $s \in \{0, 1, 2\}$ (pilot) or $\{0, 1, 2, 3, 4\}$ (final):

**Hyperparameters:**
- Optimizer: AdamW, lr = 1e-3, weight_decay = 1e-2
- Batch size: 32
- Max epochs: 100
- Early stopping: patience 25 on validation loss
- LR schedule: cosine annealing from 1e-3 to 1e-5 over max_epochs
- Gradient clipping: max norm 1.0
- Loss: BCE with logits for classification, MSE for regression

**Procedure:**
1. Initialize encoder $\theta$ and task head $\phi_i$ with seed $s$.
2. Train on $D_i$ until early stopping triggers or max epochs reached.
3. Save checkpoint at best validation loss to `checkpoints/{dataset}/stl/{task}/seed{s}.pt`.
4. Compute and save test predictions on $T_i$ to `predictions/{dataset}/stl/{task}/seed{s}.parquet`.

**Output for downstream use:** per-compound test predictions, used to compute $\text{Perf}_i^{\text{STL}}(r)$ for any region $r$ by restricting to $T_i(r)$.

### 2.5 Pairwise MTL training protocol

For each task pair $(i, j)$ and seed $s$:

**Architecture:**
- Single shared encoder $\theta$ (same architecture as STL)
- Two task heads $\phi_i, \phi_j$ (same architecture as STL heads)

**Hyperparameters:** identical to STL.

**Loss:**
$\mathcal{L}_{ij}(\theta, \phi_i, \phi_j) = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}_i|} \sum_{x \in \mathcal{B}_i} \ell_i(x; \theta, \phi_i) + \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}_j|} \sum_{x \in \mathcal{B}_j} \ell_j(x; \theta, \phi_j)$

where $\mathcal{B}_i, \mathcal{B}_j$ are the within-batch subsets with valid labels for tasks $i, j$ respectively.

**Batch construction:**
- Each batch contains compounds with valid labels for at least one of $\{i, j\}$
- Compounds with valid labels for both contribute to both task losses (joint update)
- Compounds with valid label for only one task contribute only to that task's loss

**Procedure:**
1. Initialize encoder $\theta$ and task heads $\phi_i, \phi_j$ with seed $s$.
2. Train on $D_i \cup D_j$ until early stopping (combined validation loss).
3. Save checkpoint at best combined validation loss to `checkpoints/{dataset}/mtl/{task_i}_{task_j}/seed{s}.pt`.
4. Compute and save test predictions for both tasks: `predictions/{dataset}/mtl/{task_i}_{task_j}/seed{s}.parquet` with columns for both task predictions.

### 2.6 Region partitioning

Three partitioning schemes are implemented. Scaffolds are primary; the others provide robustness checks.

#### 2.6.1 Bemis-Murcko scaffold partitioning (primary)

**Step 1:** Collect all unique scaffolds in the dataset (computed in Section 2.2 Step 3).

**Step 2:** For each scaffold, retrieve its Morgan fingerprint (Section 2.2 Step 4).

**Step 3:** Cluster scaffolds using hierarchical agglomerative clustering on Tanimoto distance:
- Distance: $1 - \text{Tanimoto}(fp_a, fp_b)$
- Linkage: average
- Number of clusters: $M \in \{3, 5, 8, 10\}$ (default $M = 5$)

**Step 4:** Assign each compound to the cluster of its scaffold. The partitioning function $\pi(x) = \text{cluster}(\text{scaffold}(x))$.

**Step 5:** Validate region sizes. If any region has fewer than $2 n_{\min}$ training compounds for any task pair under analysis, merge it with its nearest neighbor cluster (smallest centroid distance) before downstream analysis.

**Implementation:** `partition/scaffold.py`. Save partitioning to `partitions/{dataset}/scaffold_M{M}.parquet` with columns `[smiles, region_id]`.

#### 2.6.2 Latent embedding cluster partitioning (secondary)

**Step 1:** Train a single multi-task encoder on all tasks of the dataset (using all-task MTL with the standard protocol) for one seed (seed=0).

**Step 2:** Compute encoder representations $f_\theta(x) \in \mathbb{R}^{256}$ for all compounds.

**Step 3:** Cluster representations with k-means, $M \in \{3, 5, 8, 10\}$ clusters, k-means++ initialization, 10 restarts.

**Step 4:** $\pi(x) = \arg\min_r \|f_\theta(x) - \mu_r\|$.

**Implementation:** `partition/latent.py`. Save to `partitions/{dataset}/latent_M{M}.parquet`.

#### 2.6.3 kNN neighborhood partitioning (tertiary)

**Step 1:** Use scaffold cluster centroids from Section 2.6.1 as anchor points.

**Step 2:** For each compound $x$, assign it to the region of its nearest scaffold centroid in encoder representation space.

**Step 3:** $\pi(x) = \arg\min_r \|f_\theta(x) - \mu_r^{\text{scaffold}}\|$, where $\mu_r^{\text{scaffold}}$ is the mean encoder embedding of compounds whose scaffold belongs to scaffold cluster $r$.

**Implementation:** `partition/knn.py`. Save to `partitions/{dataset}/knn_M{M}.parquet`.

#### 2.6.4 Random partitioning (negative control)

**Procedure:** Generate $B = 200$ random partitions matched for region-size distribution to the primary scaffold partition.

**Step 1:** Compute the empirical region-size distribution $\{n_1, \ldots, n_M\}$ of the scaffold partition for the dataset.

**Step 2:** For each $b \in \{1, \ldots, B\}$:
- Randomly shuffle compound indices.
- Assign the first $n_1$ to region 1, next $n_2$ to region 2, etc.
- Save as `partitions/{dataset}/random_b{b}.parquet`.

**Use:** Each random partition is processed through the full pipeline (sign heterogeneity computation, predictor evaluation) to build empirical null distributions for each statistic of interest. See Section 2.13.

### 2.7 Regional gradient affinity computation

For task $i$, region $r$, and a trained pairwise MTL model with parameters $(\theta, \phi_i, \phi_j)$:

**Step 1: Subsample if needed.** If $|D_i(r)| > 500$, randomly subsample 500 compounds for gradient computation (with replacement across bootstrap iterations, see Section 2.10).

**Step 2: Compute per-task regional gradient.**
$g_i(r) = \frac{1}{|D_i(r)|} \sum_{x \in D_i(r)} \nabla_\theta \ell_i(x; \theta, \phi_i)$

The gradient is computed with respect to encoder parameters $\theta$ only (not task heads). This makes the comparison meaningful: we are measuring how the encoder is being pushed by each task within the region.

**Implementation detail:** Use `torch.autograd.grad(loss, encoder.parameters(), retain_graph=True)`. Flatten and concatenate all encoder parameter gradients into a single vector. The resulting vector has dimension equal to the encoder parameter count (~250K for the GCN).

**Step 3: Compute regional affinity.**
$G_{ij}(r) = \frac{g_i(r) \cdot g_j(r)}{\|g_i(r)\| \|g_j(r)\|}$

If either $\|g_i(r)\|$ or $\|g_j(r)\|$ is below $10^{-8}$, mark $G_{ij}(r)$ as undefined (the model has converged on this region for this task and gradients are noise).

**Step 4: Stability over training trajectory.** Compute $G_{ij}(r)$ at three checkpoints:
- Final checkpoint (best validation)
- 80% of training epochs
- 60% of training epochs

Report mean and standard deviation. Primary analysis uses the final-checkpoint value; trajectory stability is reported as a robustness check.

**Step 5: Save.** Output to `gradients/{dataset}/{task_i}_{task_j}/seed{s}/region_affinity.parquet` with columns `[region_id, G_ij, g_i_norm, g_j_norm, n_i_in_region, n_j_in_region, checkpoint_label]`.

### 2.8 Regional transfer benefit computation

For task pair $(i, j)$, region $r$, and seed $s$:

**Step 1: Restrict test set to region.** $T_i(r) = \{x \in T_i : \pi(x) = r\}$, similarly $T_j(r)$.

**Step 2: Compute regional STL performance.**
$\text{Perf}_i^{\text{STL}}(r) = \text{ROC-AUC}(\hat{y}_i^{\text{STL}}(T_i(r)), y_i(T_i(r)))$

For regression tasks, use negative RMSE (so that higher is better in all cases).

If $T_i(r)$ has fewer than 30 compounds for classification or 50 for regression, OR has only one class for classification, mark performance as undefined and exclude this region from analysis.

**Step 3: Compute regional MTL performance.** Same procedure using MTL predictions.

**Step 4: Compute regional benefits.**

Pair-averaged:
$\Delta_{ij}(r) = \text{Perf}_{ij}^{\text{MTL}}(r) - \frac{1}{2}\left[\text{Perf}_i^{\text{STL}}(r) + \text{Perf}_j^{\text{STL}}(r)\right]$

Task-specific:
$\Delta_{i \leftarrow j}(r) = \text{Perf}_{ij}^{\text{MTL}, i}(r) - \text{Perf}_i^{\text{STL}}(r)$
$\Delta_{j \leftarrow i}(r) = \text{Perf}_{ij}^{\text{MTL}, j}(r) - \text{Perf}_j^{\text{STL}}(r)$

Worst-task:
$\Delta_{ij}^{\text{worst}}(r) = \min(\Delta_{i \leftarrow j}(r), \Delta_{j \leftarrow i}(r))$

**Step 5: Aggregate across seeds.** For each $\Delta$ quantity, report mean across seeds. Bootstrap CIs (Section 2.10) capture both bootstrap variability and seed variability via two-level resampling.

**Step 6: Save.** Output to `benefits/{dataset}/{task_i}_{task_j}/region_benefits.parquet`.

### 2.9 Sign heterogeneity and cancellation indices

Computed per task pair, after benefits and CIs are available.

**Sign heterogeneity (pair-averaged):**
$S_{ij} = \mathbb{1}\left[\exists r_a \in \mathcal{R}_{\text{valid}}, r_b \in \mathcal{R}_{\text{valid}}: \Delta_{ij}(r_a) > +\epsilon \text{ and } \text{CI}_{95}(\Delta_{ij}(r_a)) \cap (-\infty, 0] = \emptyset\right.$
$\left. \text{ and } \Delta_{ij}(r_b) < -\epsilon \text{ and } \text{CI}_{95}(\Delta_{ij}(r_b)) \cap [0, +\infty) = \emptyset\right]$

In words: there exist two valid regions where one has confidently positive benefit ($> +\epsilon$ with CI excluding 0) and the other has confidently negative benefit ($< -\epsilon$ with CI excluding 0). Default $\epsilon = 1.5$ AUC points (0.10 RMSE for regression).

**Sign heterogeneity (task-specific):** $S_{ij}^i$ and $S_{ij}^j$ defined analogously using $\Delta_{i \leftarrow j}(r)$ and $\Delta_{j \leftarrow i}(r)$.

**Heterogeneity index:**
$H_{ij} = \sum_{r \in \mathcal{R}_{\text{valid}}} p(r) \left|\Delta_{ij}(r) - \bar{\Delta}_{ij}\right|$

where $p(r) = |T_i(r) \cup T_j(r)| / \sum_{r'} |T_i(r') \cup T_j(r')|$ and $\bar{\Delta}_{ij}$ is the test-set-weighted mean across regions.

**Cancellation index:**
$C_{ij} = \frac{\sum_{r \in \mathcal{R}_{\text{valid}}} p(r) |\Delta_{ij}(r)|}{\left|\sum_{r \in \mathcal{R}_{\text{valid}}} p(r) \Delta_{ij}(r)\right| + \eta}$

with $\eta = 0.5$. $C_{ij} > 1$ means regional magnitudes substantially exceed the global average.

### 2.10 Hierarchical bootstrap procedure

For computing CIs on regional quantities ($\Delta_{ij}(r)$, $G_{ij}(r)$) and aggregate statistics (heterogeneity prevalence, predictor AUROC).

**Two-level resampling:**

For $b \in \{1, \ldots, B\}$ where $B = 1000$:

1. **Cluster level:** Resample scaffold clusters (or embedding clusters) with replacement at the partition's coarsest level. For region $r$, this means resampling the set of scaffolds whose compounds belong to $r$.
2. **Compound level:** Within each resampled cluster, resample compounds with replacement.
3. Compute the statistic of interest on the resampled data.

**For $G_{ij}(r)$:** Resample compounds within region $r$ (cluster-level resampling not applicable since we're already within a single region; use within-region scaffold resampling instead). Recompute $g_i(r)$ and $g_j(r)$ on the resampled compounds. Compute $G_{ij}(r)^{(b)}$.

**For $\Delta_{ij}(r)$:** Resample test compounds within region $r$ (with within-region scaffold-level structure). Recompute $\text{Perf}_{ij}^{\text{MTL}}(r)^{(b)}$, $\text{Perf}_i^{\text{STL}}(r)^{(b)}$, $\text{Perf}_j^{\text{STL}}(r)^{(b)}$. Compute $\Delta_{ij}(r)^{(b)}$.

**For seed variability:** For each bootstrap iteration $b$, also randomly select one of the available seeds. This produces CIs that capture both data and training stochasticity.

**CI computation:** Percentile bootstrap. $\text{CI}_{95}(X) = [\text{quantile}(X^{(1:B)}, 0.025), \text{quantile}(X^{(1:B)}, 0.975)]$.

**Implementation:** `bootstrap/hierarchical.py`. The function signature is `hierarchical_bootstrap(compute_fn, data, n_iter=1000, scaffold_groups=None, seeds=None)` and returns `{"estimate": ..., "ci_lower": ..., "ci_upper": ..., "samples": ...}`.

**Pre-pilot bootstrap calibration check:** Before running the full pipeline, run hierarchical bootstrap on Tox21 with $M = 5$ scaffold regions and verify that:
- CIs for known-stable statistics (e.g., $G_{ii}(r) \approx 1$) have width < 0.05
- CIs for $\Delta_{ij}(r)$ on a known-positive task pair (NR-AR vs NR-AR-LBD) are non-degenerate
- Bootstrap distribution is approximately normal (Shapiro-Wilk p > 0.01 for at least 80% of statistics)

If these fail, debug before proceeding.

### 2.11 Local-support validity criterion

A regional affinity estimate $G_{ij}(r)$ is *valid* if all of:

1. $|D_i(r)| \geq n_{\min}$ AND $|D_j(r)| \geq n_{\min}$ (default $n_{\min} = 50$)
2. $|T_i(r)| \geq 30$ AND $|T_j(r)| \geq 30$ for classification, $\geq 50$ for regression
3. Both $T_i(r)$ and $T_j(r)$ contain at least 5 positive and 5 negative examples (classification only)
4. Hierarchical-bootstrap 95% CI on $G_{ij}(r)$ has width $\leq w_{\max}$ (default $w_{\max} = 0.4$)
5. $\|g_i(r)\| > 10^{-6}$ AND $\|g_j(r)\| > 10^{-6}$ (gradients are not numerically zero)

Invalid regions are excluded from prevalence statistics and predictor evaluation. Abstention rate is reported as a separate quantity.

### 2.12 Definition of "meaningful task pair"

A task pair $(i, j)$ enters the heterogeneity-prevalence denominator only if:

1. At least 3 valid regions (per Section 2.11)
2. At least 1 region with $|\Delta_{ij}(r)| > 1.5$ AUC (or 0.10 RMSE) AND CI excluding 0
3. Both tasks have non-degenerate label distributions in at least 3 regions
4. Test set size criteria satisfied in at least 3 regions

This locks the denominator before analysis. Save the meaningful-pair list to `analysis/{dataset}/meaningful_pairs.parquet` after Phase 1 measurements.

### 2.13 Random-partition null distribution

For each dataset, partition resolution $M$, and statistic $S$ (where $S$ ranges over heterogeneity prevalence, mean $|H_{ij}|$, mean $C_{ij}$, predictor AUROC):

**Step 1:** Compute $S$ under the scaffold partition: $S^{\text{scaffold}}$.

**Step 2:** For each random partition $b \in \{1, \ldots, 200\}$ (Section 2.6.4), compute $S^{(b)}$.

**Step 3:** Empirical p-value:
$p_S = \frac{1 + \#\{b : S^{(b)} \geq S^{\text{scaffold}}\}}{1 + B}$

The phenomenon is established for statistic $S$ if $p_S < 0.05$.

**Step 4:** Report null distribution as violin/box plot in Figure 3 alongside scaffold-partition value.

**Implementation:** `null_distribution/random_partition.py`. Save full null distribution arrays to `analysis/{dataset}/null_dist_{statistic}_M{M}.npy`.

### 2.14 Predictor evaluation protocol

For each predictor method (Section 7.3 lists all 10), compute:

**Inputs available per (task pair, region):**
- $G_{ij}(r)$ (local gradient cosine)
- TAG-style local affinity (if used)
- Scaffold similarity within region (mean Tanimoto between $D_i(r)$ scaffolds and $D_j(r)$ scaffolds)
- Regional embedding distance ($\|\text{mean}(f_\theta(D_i(r))) - \text{mean}(f_\theta(D_j(r)))\|_2$)
- Regional label correlation (Pearson on co-measured compounds in $r$, when available)
- Regional MMD between $\{f_\theta(x) : x \in D_i(r)\}$ and $\{f_\theta(x) : x \in D_j(r)\}$
- Local support sizes $|D_i(r)|, |D_j(r)|$

**Targets:**
- $\text{sign}(\Delta_{ij}(r))$ (binary, +1 / -1; regions with $|\Delta| < 0.5$ AUC marked "neutral" and excluded)
- $\Delta_{ij}(r)$ (continuous)
- Same for task-specific benefits

**Evaluation procedure:**

For predictors that take a single feature (e.g., $G_{ij}(r)$ alone):
- Threshold-based AUROC: vary threshold on the feature, compute ROC.
- Spearman correlation with continuous target.

For predictors that combine features (e.g., joint logistic regression):
- 5-fold leave-task-pairs-out cross-validation: holding out 20% of task pairs (with all their regions) per fold.
- Train logistic regression on training pairs, predict regional sign on held-out pairs.
- Compute AUROC, AUPRC, balanced accuracy on held-out predictions, aggregated across folds.
- Report calibration: ECE with 10 bins, reliability diagram.

**Incremental $R^2$ test (CENTERPIECE):**

Run two regressions on continuous target $\Delta_{ij}(r)$:

**Baseline:** $\Delta_{ij}(r) \sim \text{ScaffoldSim}(r) + \text{LabelCorr}(r) + \text{EmbeddingDist}(r) + \log(|D_i(r)|) + \log(|D_j(r)|)$

**Full:** baseline + $G_{ij}(r) + G_{ij}(r)^2$

Report $R^2_{\text{full}} - R^2_{\text{baseline}}$ with bootstrap CI. Also report partial F-test on the addition of $G_{ij}(r)$ terms.

This is the primary scientific test for whether gradient affinity adds value beyond chemistry.

**Implementation:** `predictor/eval.py`.

## Part 3: Risk Register and Mitigations

### Risk 1 (PRIMARY SCIENTIFIC RISK): Scaffold similarity explains everything

**Probability:** Medium-high. Two regions in the same scaffold cluster are by definition chemically similar; this might fully explain regional transfer patterns and reduce the gradient-affinity contribution to noise.

**Why this is the primary risk:** If true, the paper has no methodological contribution. The phenomenon would still exist, but the predictor's added value over a chemistry baseline would be zero, which would force a workshop paper.

**Detection:** In Phase 2, run joint regression $\Delta_{ij}(r) \sim \text{ScaffoldSim}_{ij}(r) + G_{ij}(r) + \text{LabelCorr}_{ij}(r)$ and report incremental $R^2$ and partial correlation of each predictor. This is a centerpiece result, not an aside.

**Mitigation:**
- Latent embedding partitioning as alternative (decouples chemical similarity from learned task structure)
- Within-scaffold-cluster analysis showing $G_{ij}(r)$ varies meaningfully even when scaffold structure is held constant
- Report incremental $R^2$ for $G_{ij}(r)$ over chemistry baselines as a primary result
- Consider that gradient affinity may capture *task-specific* representational alignment that scaffold similarity cannot, especially across diverse molecular families

**Kill criterion:** $G_{ij}(r)$ contributes less than $0.05$ incremental $R^2$ over scaffold similarity in joint regression, across multiple datasets. Reframe paper around scaffold-based regional structure rather than gradient affinity, and pivot to workshop submission.

### Risk 2: Sign heterogeneity is rare or absent

**Probability:** Medium. Plausible that MTL benefit is roughly monotonic across regions for most pairs.

**Detection:** Pilot Phase 0 directly measures prevalence.

**Mitigation:**
- Test deliberately weakly-related task pairs (cross-domain Tox21+ADME) where heterogeneity is more likely than for tightly-related pairs
- Test high-overlap datasets first to remove confounds
- Define meaningful effect sizes upfront (the 1.5 AUC threshold)
- Use sufficient region partitioning resolution (3-8 regions per dataset)
- Examine task-specific (asymmetric) heterogeneity as a separate channel — even pairs that look symmetric may show asymmetric regional transfer

**Kill criterion:** Fewer than 5 task pairs across all datasets show stable sign heterogeneity passing the CI test. Project becomes a workshop paper or is dropped.

### Risk 3: Local gradient affinity does not predict regional transfer sign

**Probability:** Medium-low. Your ICLR paper showed global cosine works above 30% overlap; locally restricted versions should also work given enough samples per region.

**Detection:** Phase 0 pilot measures regional sign prediction AUROC.

**Mitigation:**
- TAG-style affinity as backup metric (pre-registered as ablation)
- Last-shared-layer gradients vs. all-encoder gradients (tested as ablation)
- Fisher-normalized gradients (tested as ablation)
- Larger region sizes if small-region noise dominates

**Kill criterion:** Local gradient affinity AUROC for regional sign prediction does not exceed best non-gradient baseline (scaffold similarity, regional label correlation, embedding distance) by at least 0.07. Project drops to workshop paper.

### Risk 4: Reviewers see this as DynaShare/MMoE rebranded

**Probability:** Medium. The most likely reviewer attack.

**Mitigation:**
- Frame the contribution as diagnosis and measurement, not as a competing architecture
- Include MMoE and one other adaptive sharing baseline (Cross-Stitch, Sluice, or DynaShare if implementation is clean) as performance baselines
- Show calibrated regional sign prediction as something routing methods do not provide (their gating weights are not designed to predict regional MTL benefit)
- Demonstrate that local gradient affinity, computed before MTL training, predicts what routing methods learn during training. This positions local affinity as a *predictor* and *diagnostic*, not a competitor

### Risk 5: Region partitioning is itself a confound

**Probability:** Medium. Different partitionings might give different sign-heterogeneity prevalence.

**Mitigation:**
- Test all three partitioning schemes (scaffolds, latent clusters, kNN)
- Report agreement: phenomenon must hold across at least two
- Random-partition negative control with proper null distribution: scaffold-based prevalence must exceed 95th percentile of random-partition null
- Vary number of regions (3, 5, 8, 10) and report stability

### Risk 6: Small-region statistical noise

**Probability:** High. Some regions will have small samples even after the $n_{\min}$ threshold.

**Mitigation:**
- Hierarchical bootstrap CIs everywhere
- Abstention via the CI-width criterion
- Multiple seeds (5 final) and averaging
- Explicit noise-floor experiment: shuffle labels within regions and show $G_{ij}(r)$ becomes noise

### Risk 7: Compute cost

**Probability:** Medium. Pairwise MTL training across many task pairs, multiple seeds, multiple architectures is expensive.

**Mitigation:**
- 2-4 H100 GPUs per available tooling
- Reuse trained encoders where possible
- Pilot uses 3 seeds; final uses 5
- Limit final task-pair count to ~80-120 across all datasets
- Stage compute: don't run final architecture variants until pilot greenlights

### Risk 8: Phenomenon exists but is too dataset-specific

**Probability:** Medium-low. Phenomenon should generalize across molecular datasets, but might not generalize beyond chemistry.

**Mitigation:**
- Own the molecular scope explicitly. The paper is about sparse molecular MTL with chemical structure.
- Briefly speculate on generalizability in discussion without claiming it
- Future work section discusses extension to other domains

## Part 5: Phase 0 — Pilot

### 5.1 Goal

Decide whether the project is worth pursuing. Test the phenomenon claim and the predictor claim with minimal infrastructure.

### 5.2 Datasets

Tox21 (12 tasks, 100% overlap), SIDER (27 tasks, 100% overlap). High overlap removes the support-availability confound at this stage.

### 5.3 Task pairs

20-40 task pairs sampled to include:
- Within-mechanism pairs (e.g., NR vs. NR pathways in Tox21) — expected positive transfer
- Cross-mechanism pairs (NR vs. SR) — likely heterogeneous
- SOC-grouped vs. cross-SOC pairs in SIDER

### 5.4 Architecture

Reuse ICLR paper's GCN architecture: 3-layer GCN, hidden dim 256, global mean pooling, MLP heads. AdamW, lr 1e-3, batch 32, 30 epochs, early stopping on validation.

### 5.5 Region partitioning

Bemis-Murcko scaffolds. Cluster scaffolds into 3-5 groups using hierarchical clustering on Tanimoto similarity of scaffold fingerprints. Require minimum 50 compounds per region.

### 5.6 Experimental protocol

For each dataset:

1. Train STL models for each task. 3 seeds. Save checkpoints.
2. Train pairwise MTL models for each task pair. 3 seeds. Save checkpoints.
3. For each pair × region: compute $\Delta_{ij}(r)$, $\Delta_{i \leftarrow j}(r)$, $\Delta_{j \leftarrow i}(r)$ on test split with hierarchical-bootstrap 95% CI.
4. For each pair × region: compute $G_{ij}(r)$ from gradients on training compounds in region. Hierarchical-bootstrap CI on $G_{ij}(r)$.
5. For each pair: compute $S_{ij}$ (sign heterogeneity, pair and task-specific), $H_{ij}$ (heterogeneity index), $C_{ij}$ (cancellation).
6. Test predictor: across all valid regions, does $G_{ij}(r)$ predict $\text{sign}(\Delta_{ij}(r))$? Compute AUROC and Spearman.
7. Build random-partition null distributions (200 random partitions per dataset) and compare scaffold-based metrics against the null.
8. Filter task pairs by the four-criteria "meaningful pair" definition before computing prevalence.

### 5.7 Green-light criteria (BOTH required)

**Phenomenon check:**
- At least 5 task pairs across the two datasets show stable sign heterogeneity (passes $|\Delta| > 1.5$ AUC threshold AND CI test in at least one positive and one negative region)
- AND at least 15% of meaningful task pairs (per the four-criteria definition in Section 1.3) exhibit sign heterogeneity
- AND scaffold-based heterogeneity prevalence exceeds the 95th percentile of the random-partition null distribution

**Predictor check:**
- Local gradient affinity AUROC for regional sign prediction $\geq 0.70$
- AND local gradient affinity AUROC exceeds best non-gradient baseline (scaffold similarity, regional label correlation, embedding distance) by at least 0.07
- AND Spearman $\rho$ between $G_{ij}(r)$ and $\Delta_{ij}(r) \geq 0.45$
- AND results stable across 3 seeds (sign of $G_{ij}(r)$ agrees in $\geq 80\%$ of regions across seeds)

### 5.8 Decision tree

- **Both pass:** Full project proceed. NeurIPS target.
- **Phenomenon passes, predictor fails:** Workshop paper on phenomenon discovery; or invest in better predictors (TAG-style, Fisher-normalized) before deciding.
- **Phenomenon fails, predictor untestable:** Drop project or pivot to "conditions under which global affinity is sufficient" framing (much weaker, probably workshop only).
- **Both fail:** Drop project.

### 5.9 Pilot deliverables

- Sign heterogeneity prevalence table across datasets, with random-partition null comparison
- Pair-averaged AND task-specific regional benefit tables
- Predictor AUROC comparison vs. baselines
- One compelling task-pair example for Figure 2
- Hierarchical-bootstrap stability assessment
- Decision document with explicit go/no-go reasoning

## Part 8: Phase 3 — Utility Evaluation

This phase establishes practical value: using local affinity to make sharing decisions. Real architectures, real baselines, honest comparison.

### 8.1 Goal

Show that local-affinity-guided sharing reduces regional negative transfer while preserving regional positive transfer, and either matches or beats existing routing methods.

### 8.2 Sharing strategies compared

**Single-task baselines:**
1. STL: train each task independently

**Static all-task baselines:**
2. All-task MTL: train one shared encoder on all tasks
3. Pairwise MTL union: train pairwise MTL for each pair, ensemble at test

**Global grouping baselines:**
4. Random grouping (10 trials averaged)
5. Global gradient affinity grouping (your ICLR paper)
6. TAG-style affinity grouping (Fifty et al. or close re-implementation)

**Adaptive sharing baselines:**
7. MMoE: mixture-of-experts with task-specific gates
8. One additional adaptive sharing method: DynaShare if implementation is clean and faithful, otherwise Cross-Stitch Networks (Misra et al.) or Sluice Networks (Ruder et al.). Decision made before Phase 3 begins based on engineering assessment. Bad implementation is worse than no implementation; the baseline must be defensible.

**Local affinity strategies (the proposed approach):**
9. Local affinity hard sharing: For each region, pre-compute $G_{ij}(r)$ for all task pairs. Train region-conditional MTL where shared parameters are active only between task pairs with $G_{ij}(r) > \tau_+$ in that region.
10. Local affinity soft sharing: Use $G_{ij}(r)$ as a continuous gate weight on cross-task parameter sharing per region.
11. Local affinity with abstention: Like (9), but for low-support regions, default to STL prediction.

### 8.3 Implementation of local affinity sharing architecture

The proposed architecture must be implemented carefully. One reasonable approach:

- Shared encoder produces representation $\phi(x)$
- Region assignment function $r(x)$ assigns each compound to a region (scaffold-based, deterministic)
- Task heads receive $\phi(x)$ plus region identity
- For each task pair $(i, j)$ and region $r$, a learned binary gate $\gamma_{ij}(r)$ controls whether encoder gradients from task $i$ flow back through shared parameters when task $j$ is being trained on region $r$
- $\gamma_{ij}(r)$ is initialized using $G_{ij}(r)$ pre-computed from a short pretraining run, then optionally fine-tuned

This is a significant engineering effort. The paper includes a clean public implementation.

### 8.4 Datasets

- Tox21, SIDER, ToxCast (high overlap)
- TDC ADMET, kinase panel (sparse)

### 8.5 Metrics

**Performance metrics:**
- Average per-task AUC/RMSE (across tasks)
- Worst-region performance (per task, then averaged)
- Average regional performance
- Sample-weighted average performance

**Negative transfer metrics:**
- Negative transfer rate: fraction of (task, region) pairs where method underperforms STL by at least 1.5 AUC
- Severe negative transfer rate: underperforms by at least 3 AUC
- Average regional negative transfer magnitude
- Task-specific negative transfer rate (using $\Delta_{i \leftarrow j}$ rather than pair average)

**Decision-quality metrics:**
- Calibration of share/separate decisions: when method predicts "share is beneficial," how often is it correct?
- Reliability diagrams for share/separate predictions
- Expected calibration error (ECE) on share/separate decisions
- Held-out task-pair calibration: fit decision rule on training task pairs, evaluate calibration on held-out pairs
- AUROC of sharing decisions vs. ground-truth regional benefit

**Efficiency metrics:**
- Training compute (GPU-hours)
- Inference latency
- Number of effective parameters

### 8.6 Statistical comparison

Pairwise comparisons between methods using paired tests across (task, region) combinations. Bonferroni correction across the comparison family. Hierarchical-bootstrap CIs on all reported numbers.

### 8.7 Expected outcomes and pre-committed interpretation

- If local affinity sharing matches or beats DynaShare/MMoE on average performance AND has substantially lower negative transfer rate: strong result
- If local affinity sharing matches DynaShare/MMoE on average but is interpretable while they are not: moderate result, framed around interpretability and calibration
- If local affinity sharing substantially underperforms DynaShare/MMoE on average: still publishable as a diagnostic, but the contribution shifts

The paper must commit to honesty here. If routing methods beat local affinity on raw performance, the paper says so and reframes the contribution around regional diagnosis and calibrated abstention rather than claiming SOTA.

- Negative transfer rate comparison (key figure)

### 9.6 Negative controls

- Random label permutation: shuffle task labels within each region. $G_{ij}(r)$ should drop to noise.
- Random region assignment: matched-size random partitions, full null distribution. Should not produce sign heterogeneity at the rate observed under scaffolds.
- Single-task identity check: $G_{ii}(r) \approx 1$ for all $r$, validating the metric.

### 9.7 Number of regions

Vary M = 3, 5, 8, 10. Heterogeneity prevalence and predictor AUROC should be relatively stable; sharp dependence would suggest overfitting to partition.

## Part 10: Phase 5 — Theory and Discussion

### 10.1 Theoretical contributions

The paper does not claim a major theorem but includes:

- A formal definition of region-conditional task affinity
- A statement (with proof sketch in appendix) that under structured covariate shift across regions, the global gradient affinity is a function of (a) regional affinities, (b) regional sample weights, (c) the cosine geometry — and is not in general a weighted average of regional affinities
- A signal-to-noise analysis of when $G_{ij}(r)$ is a reliable estimator: depends on per-region sample size, gradient norm, and noise structure
- Conditions under which local-affinity-guided sharing is provably beneficial in expectation (under simplified assumptions)

These are framed as supporting analysis, not primary contributions. The paper is empirical.

### 10.2 Connection to ICLR paper

Explicit section showing the ICLR paper's overlap requirement is a special case. When tasks have full overlap (Tox21, SIDER), the global gradient affinity is reliable because all compounds are shared. When overlap is partial, regions of the input space have asymmetric task coverage, and global affinity becomes a function of which regions happen to be sampled. The NeurIPS paper generalizes the ICLR result by showing that even at full overlap, regional structure can make global affinity misleading. We generalize the principle from global sample overlap to regional support-validity.

### 10.3 When global affinity is sufficient

The paper explicitly identifies regimes where global affinity is fine:
- High-overlap datasets where all task pairs have positive (or all negative) transfer everywhere
- Task pairs where regional effects are roughly uniform
- Coarse-grained planning where regional decisions are not actionable

This honest framing strengthens the paper.

### 10.4 Limitations

- Molecular focus: extension to vision/NLP is future work
- Region partitioning is itself a modeling choice; the paper doesn't fully solve "what is the right region structure"
- Local-affinity sharing architecture has computational overhead vs. all-task MTL
- Regional sign prediction requires sufficient per-region samples; very sparse settings remain challenging
- Hierarchical bootstrap assumes scaffold groupings have approximately exchangeable substructure within clusters

## Part 12: Paper Structure

### 12.1 Abstract

Multi-task learning methods typically treat task compatibility as a global relationship between task pairs. We show this assumption can fail: the same pair of molecular prediction tasks can exhibit positive transfer in one chemical region and negative transfer in another. This *transfer sign heterogeneity* causes global task-affinity scores to cancel opposing regional effects and mislead task grouping. We define region-conditional transfer benefit, introduce support-valid regional gradient affinity as a diagnostic, and show that local gradient affinity predicts regional transfer beyond scaffold similarity and label-correlation baselines. Across five molecular datasets, sign heterogeneity is common (X% of meaningful task pairs, exceeding random-partition null at p<0.05) and produces measurable cancellation in global affinity. Local gradient affinity predicts regional transfer sign with AUROC Y, adding incremental $R^2$ of Z over chemistry baselines. Using local affinity for share/separate/abstain decisions reduces regional negative transfer by W% while matching adaptive sharing on average performance. Our results suggest task affinity should not be treated as a scalar relationship in domains where transfer is regionally heterogeneous.

### 12.2 Introduction (1.5 pages)

Lead with the phenomenon, not the method:

- MTL needs task compatibility decisions. Existing methods (TAG, DynaShare, MMoE) provide task-pair-level affinity scores or implicit instance-level routing.
- These methods share an unstated assumption: task compatibility is a property of the task pair. We show this assumption can fail.
- Concrete example: introduce one canonical sign-heterogeneous task pair from results, with the regional benefit figure inline.
- Generalization: across five datasets, this happens often enough that global task affinity is systematically misleading.
- The ICLR paper showed gradient affinity requires shared samples. We show that even with shared samples, the same pair can show opposite transfer in different regions, generalizing the principle from sample overlap to regional support-validity.
- Contribution stack:
  1. **Phenomenon**: define and measure transfer sign heterogeneity (pair-averaged and task-specific)
  2. **Failure mode**: characterize global affinity cancellation
  3. **Diagnostic**: region-conditional gradient affinity with explicit support-validity, evaluated against chemistry baselines
  4. **Utility**: local-affinity sharing reduces negative transfer
  5. **Robustness**: phenomenon holds across architectures, partitionings, and sample sizes
- Field-level implication: task compatibility is sign-changing, support-dependent, and region-conditional. Single-scalar task affinity is sufficient only when transfer is regionally stable.

## Part 3: Risk Register and Mitigations

### Risk 1 (PRIMARY SCIENTIFIC RISK): Scaffold similarity explains everything

**Probability:** Medium-high. Two regions in the same scaffold cluster are by definition chemically similar; this might fully explain regional transfer patterns and reduce the gradient-affinity contribution to noise.

**Why this is the primary risk:** If true, the paper has no methodological contribution. The phenomenon would still exist, but the predictor's added value over a chemistry baseline would be zero, which would force a workshop paper.

**Detection:** In Phase 2, run joint regression $\Delta_{ij}(r) \sim \text{ScaffoldSim}_{ij}(r) + G_{ij}(r) + \text{LabelCorr}_{ij}(r)$ and report incremental $R^2$ and partial correlation of each predictor. This is a centerpiece result, not an aside.

**Mitigation:**
- Latent embedding partitioning as alternative (decouples chemical similarity from learned task structure)
- Within-scaffold-cluster analysis showing $G_{ij}(r)$ varies meaningfully even when scaffold structure is held constant
- Report incremental $R^2$ for $G_{ij}(r)$ over chemistry baselines as a primary result
- Consider that gradient affinity may capture *task-specific* representational alignment that scaffold similarity cannot, especially across diverse molecular families

**Kill criterion:** $G_{ij}(r)$ contributes less than $0.05$ incremental $R^2$ over scaffold similarity in joint regression, across multiple datasets. Reframe paper around scaffold-based regional structure rather than gradient affinity, and pivot to workshop submission.

### Risk 2: Sign heterogeneity is rare or absent

**Probability:** Medium. Plausible that MTL benefit is roughly monotonic across regions for most pairs.

**Detection:** Pilot Phase 0 directly measures prevalence.

**Mitigation:**
- Test deliberately weakly-related task pairs (cross-domain Tox21+ADME) where heterogeneity is more likely than for tightly-related pairs
- Test high-overlap datasets first to remove confounds
- Define meaningful effect sizes upfront (the 1.5 AUC threshold)
- Use sufficient region partitioning resolution (3-8 regions per dataset)
- Examine task-specific (asymmetric) heterogeneity as a separate channel — even pairs that look symmetric may show asymmetric regional transfer

**Kill criterion:** Fewer than 5 task pairs across all datasets show stable sign heterogeneity passing the CI test. Project becomes a workshop paper or is dropped.

### Risk 3: Local gradient affinity does not predict regional transfer sign

**Probability:** Medium-low. Your ICLR paper showed global cosine works above 30% overlap; locally restricted versions should also work given enough samples per region.

**Detection:** Phase 0 pilot measures regional sign prediction AUROC.

**Mitigation:**
- TAG-style affinity as backup metric (pre-registered as ablation)
- Last-shared-layer gradients vs. all-encoder gradients (tested as ablation)
- Fisher-normalized gradients (tested as ablation)
- Larger region sizes if small-region noise dominates

**Kill criterion:** Local gradient affinity AUROC for regional sign prediction does not exceed best non-gradient baseline (scaffold similarity, regional label correlation, embedding distance) by at least 0.07. Project drops to workshop paper.

### Risk 4: Reviewers see this as DynaShare/MMoE rebranded

**Probability:** Medium. The most likely reviewer attack.

**Mitigation:**
- Frame the contribution as diagnosis and measurement, not as a competing architecture
- Include MMoE and one other adaptive sharing baseline (Cross-Stitch, Sluice, or DynaShare if implementation is clean) as performance baselines
- Show calibrated regional sign prediction as something routing methods do not provide (their gating weights are not designed to predict regional MTL benefit)
- Demonstrate that local gradient affinity, computed before MTL training, predicts what routing methods learn during training. This positions local affinity as a *predictor* and *diagnostic*, not a competitor

### Risk 5: Region partitioning is itself a confound

**Probability:** Medium. Different partitionings might give different sign-heterogeneity prevalence.

**Mitigation:**
- Test all three partitioning schemes (scaffolds, latent clusters, kNN)
- Report agreement: phenomenon must hold across at least two
- Random-partition negative control with proper null distribution: scaffold-based prevalence must exceed 95th percentile of random-partition null
- Vary number of regions (3, 5, 8, 10) and report stability

### Risk 6: Small-region statistical noise

**Probability:** High. Some regions will have small samples even after the $n_{\min}$ threshold.

**Mitigation:**
- Hierarchical bootstrap CIs everywhere
- Abstention via the CI-width criterion
- Multiple seeds (5 final) and averaging
- Explicit noise-floor experiment: shuffle labels within regions and show $G_{ij}(r)$ becomes noise

### Risk 7: Compute cost

**Probability:** Medium. Pairwise MTL training across many task pairs, multiple seeds, multiple architectures is expensive.

**Mitigation:**
- 2-4 H100 GPUs per available tooling
- Reuse trained encoders where possible
- Pilot uses 3 seeds; final uses 5
- Limit final task-pair count to ~80-120 across all datasets
- Stage compute: don't run final architecture variants until pilot greenlights

### Risk 8: Phenomenon exists but is too dataset-specific

**Probability:** Medium-low. Phenomenon should generalize across molecular datasets, but might not generalize beyond chemistry.

**Mitigation:**
- Own the molecular scope explicitly. The paper is about sparse molecular MTL with chemical structure.
- Briefly speculate on generalizability in discussion without claiming it
- Future work section discusses extension to other domains

## Part 5: Phase 0 — Pilot

### 5.1 Goal

Decide whether the project is worth pursuing. Test the phenomenon claim and the predictor claim with minimal infrastructure.

### 5.2 Datasets

Tox21 (12 tasks, 100% overlap), SIDER (27 tasks, 100% overlap). High overlap removes the support-availability confound at this stage.

### 5.3 Task pairs

20-40 task pairs sampled to include:
- Within-mechanism pairs (e.g., NR vs. NR pathways in Tox21) — expected positive transfer
- Cross-mechanism pairs (NR vs. SR) — likely heterogeneous
- SOC-grouped vs. cross-SOC pairs in SIDER

### 5.4 Architecture

Reuse ICLR paper's GCN architecture: 3-layer GCN, hidden dim 256, global mean pooling, MLP heads. AdamW, lr 1e-3, batch 32, 30 epochs, early stopping on validation.

### 5.5 Region partitioning

Bemis-Murcko scaffolds. Cluster scaffolds into 3-5 groups using hierarchical clustering on Tanimoto similarity of scaffold fingerprints. Require minimum 50 compounds per region.

### 5.6 Experimental protocol

For each dataset:

1. Train STL models for each task. 3 seeds. Save checkpoints.
2. Train pairwise MTL models for each task pair. 3 seeds. Save checkpoints.
3. For each pair × region: compute $\Delta_{ij}(r)$, $\Delta_{i \leftarrow j}(r)$, $\Delta_{j \leftarrow i}(r)$ on test split with hierarchical-bootstrap 95% CI.
4. For each pair × region: compute $G_{ij}(r)$ from gradients on training compounds in region. Hierarchical-bootstrap CI on $G_{ij}(r)$.
5. For each pair: compute $S_{ij}$ (sign heterogeneity, pair and task-specific), $H_{ij}$ (heterogeneity index), $C_{ij}$ (cancellation).
6. Test predictor: across all valid regions, does $G_{ij}(r)$ predict $\text{sign}(\Delta_{ij}(r))$? Compute AUROC and Spearman.
7. Build random-partition null distributions (200 random partitions per dataset) and compare scaffold-based metrics against the null.
8. Filter task pairs by the four-criteria "meaningful pair" definition before computing prevalence.

### 5.7 Green-light criteria (BOTH required)

**Phenomenon check:**
- At least 5 task pairs across the two datasets show stable sign heterogeneity (passes $|\Delta| > 1.5$ AUC threshold AND CI test in at least one positive and one negative region)
- AND at least 15% of meaningful task pairs (per the four-criteria definition in Section 1.3) exhibit sign heterogeneity
- AND scaffold-based heterogeneity prevalence exceeds the 95th percentile of the random-partition null distribution

**Predictor check:**
- Local gradient affinity AUROC for regional sign prediction $\geq 0.70$
- AND local gradient affinity AUROC exceeds best non-gradient baseline (scaffold similarity, regional label correlation, embedding distance) by at least 0.07
- AND Spearman $\rho$ between $G_{ij}(r)$ and $\Delta_{ij}(r) \geq 0.45$
- AND results stable across 3 seeds (sign of $G_{ij}(r)$ agrees in $\geq 80\%$ of regions across seeds)

### 5.8 Decision tree

- **Both pass:** Full project proceed. NeurIPS target.
- **Phenomenon passes, predictor fails:** Workshop paper on phenomenon discovery; or invest in better predictors (TAG-style, Fisher-normalized) before deciding.
- **Phenomenon fails, predictor untestable:** Drop project or pivot to "conditions under which global affinity is sufficient" framing (much weaker, probably workshop only).
- **Both fail:** Drop project.

### 5.9 Pilot deliverables

- Sign heterogeneity prevalence table across datasets, with random-partition null comparison
- Pair-averaged AND task-specific regional benefit tables
- Predictor AUROC comparison vs. baselines
- One compelling task-pair example for Figure 2
- Hierarchical-bootstrap stability assessment
- Decision document with explicit go/no-go reasoning

## Part 8: Phase 3 — Utility Evaluation

This phase establishes practical value: using local affinity to make sharing decisions. Real architectures, real baselines, honest comparison.

### 8.1 Goal

Show that local-affinity-guided sharing reduces regional negative transfer while preserving regional positive transfer, and either matches or beats existing routing methods.

### 8.2 Sharing strategies compared

**Single-task baselines:**
1. STL: train each task independently

**Static all-task baselines:**
2. All-task MTL: train one shared encoder on all tasks
3. Pairwise MTL union: train pairwise MTL for each pair, ensemble at test

**Global grouping baselines:**
4. Random grouping (10 trials averaged)
5. Global gradient affinity grouping (your ICLR paper)
6. TAG-style affinity grouping (Fifty et al. or close re-implementation)

**Adaptive sharing baselines:**
7. MMoE: mixture-of-experts with task-specific gates
8. One additional adaptive sharing method: DynaShare if implementation is clean and faithful, otherwise Cross-Stitch Networks (Misra et al.) or Sluice Networks (Ruder et al.). Decision made before Phase 3 begins based on engineering assessment. Bad implementation is worse than no implementation; the baseline must be defensible.

**Local affinity strategies (the proposed approach):**
9. Local affinity hard sharing: For each region, pre-compute $G_{ij}(r)$ for all task pairs. Train region-conditional MTL where shared parameters are active only between task pairs with $G_{ij}(r) > \tau_+$ in that region.
10. Local affinity soft sharing: Use $G_{ij}(r)$ as a continuous gate weight on cross-task parameter sharing per region.
11. Local affinity with abstention: Like (9), but for low-support regions, default to STL prediction.

### 8.3 Implementation of local affinity sharing architecture

The proposed architecture must be implemented carefully. One reasonable approach:

- Shared encoder produces representation $\phi(x)$
- Region assignment function $r(x)$ assigns each compound to a region (scaffold-based, deterministic)
- Task heads receive $\phi(x)$ plus region identity
- For each task pair $(i, j)$ and region $r$, a learned binary gate $\gamma_{ij}(r)$ controls whether encoder gradients from task $i$ flow back through shared parameters when task $j$ is being trained on region $r$
- $\gamma_{ij}(r)$ is initialized using $G_{ij}(r)$ pre-computed from a short pretraining run, then optionally fine-tuned

This is a significant engineering effort. The paper includes a clean public implementation.

### 8.4 Datasets

- Tox21, SIDER, ToxCast (high overlap)
- TDC ADMET, kinase panel (sparse)

### 8.5 Metrics

**Performance metrics:**
- Average per-task AUC/RMSE (across tasks)
- Worst-region performance (per task, then averaged)
- Average regional performance
- Sample-weighted average performance

**Negative transfer metrics:**
- Negative transfer rate: fraction of (task, region) pairs where method underperforms STL by at least 1.5 AUC
- Severe negative transfer rate: underperforms by at least 3 AUC
- Average regional negative transfer magnitude
- Task-specific negative transfer rate (using $\Delta_{i \leftarrow j}$ rather than pair average)

**Decision-quality metrics:**
- Calibration of share/separate decisions: when method predicts "share is beneficial," how often is it correct?
- Reliability diagrams for share/separate predictions
- Expected calibration error (ECE) on share/separate decisions
- Held-out task-pair calibration: fit decision rule on training task pairs, evaluate calibration on held-out pairs
- AUROC of sharing decisions vs. ground-truth regional benefit

**Efficiency metrics:**
- Training compute (GPU-hours)
- Inference latency
- Number of effective parameters

### 8.6 Statistical comparison

Pairwise comparisons between methods using paired tests across (task, region) combinations. Bonferroni correction across the comparison family. Hierarchical-bootstrap CIs on all reported numbers.

## Part 10: Phase 5 — Theory and Discussion

### 10.1 Theoretical contributions

The paper does not claim a major theorem but includes:

- A formal definition of region-conditional task affinity
- A statement (with proof sketch in appendix) that under structured covariate shift across regions, the global gradient affinity is a function of (a) regional affinities, (b) regional sample weights, (c) the cosine geometry — and is not in general a weighted average of regional affinities
- A signal-to-noise analysis of when $G_{ij}(r)$ is a reliable estimator: depends on per-region sample size, gradient norm, and noise structure
- Conditions under which local-affinity-guided sharing is provably beneficial in expectation (under simplified assumptions)

These are framed as supporting analysis, not primary contributions. The paper is empirical.

### 10.2 Connection to ICLR paper

Explicit section showing the ICLR paper's overlap requirement is a special case. When tasks have full overlap (Tox21, SIDER), the global gradient affinity is reliable because all compounds are shared. When overlap is partial, regions of the input space have asymmetric task coverage, and global affinity becomes a function of which regions happen to be sampled. The NeurIPS paper generalizes the ICLR result by showing that even at full overlap, regional structure can make global affinity misleading. We generalize the principle from global sample overlap to regional support-validity.

### 10.3 When global affinity is sufficient

The paper explicitly identifies regimes where global affinity is fine:
- High-overlap datasets where all task pairs have positive (or all negative) transfer everywhere
- Task pairs where regional effects are roughly uniform
- Coarse-grained planning where regional decisions are not actionable

This honest framing strengthens the paper.

### 10.4 Limitations

- Molecular focus: extension to vision/NLP is future work
- Region partitioning is itself a modeling choice; the paper doesn't fully solve "what is the right region structure"
- Local-affinity sharing architecture has computational overhead vs. all-task MTL
- Regional sign prediction requires sufficient per-region samples; very sparse settings remain challenging
- Hierarchical bootstrap assumes scaffold groupings have approximately exchangeable substructure within clusters

## Part 12: Paper Structure

### 12.1 Abstract

Multi-task learning methods typically treat task compatibility as a global relationship between task pairs. We show this assumption can fail: the same pair of molecular prediction tasks can exhibit positive transfer in one chemical region and negative transfer in another. This *transfer sign heterogeneity* causes global task-affinity scores to cancel opposing regional effects and mislead task grouping. We define region-conditional transfer benefit, introduce support-valid regional gradient affinity as a diagnostic, and show that local gradient affinity predicts regional transfer beyond scaffold similarity and label-correlation baselines. Across five molecular datasets, sign heterogeneity is common (X% of meaningful task pairs, exceeding random-partition null at p<0.05) and produces measurable cancellation in global affinity. Local gradient affinity predicts regional transfer sign with AUROC Y, adding incremental $R^2$ of Z over chemistry baselines. Using local affinity for share/separate/abstain decisions reduces regional negative transfer by W% while matching adaptive sharing on average performance. Our results suggest task affinity should not be treated as a scalar relationship in domains where transfer is regionally heterogeneous.

### 12.2 Introduction (1.5 pages)

Lead with the phenomenon, not the method:

- MTL needs task compatibility decisions. Existing methods (TAG, DynaShare, MMoE) provide task-pair-level affinity scores or implicit instance-level routing.
- These methods share an unstated assumption: task compatibility is a property of the task pair. We show this assumption can fail.
- Concrete example: introduce one canonical sign-heterogeneous task pair from results, with the regional benefit figure inline.
- Generalization: across five datasets, this happens often enough that global task affinity is systematically misleading.
- The ICLR paper showed gradient affinity requires shared samples. We show that even with shared samples, the same pair can show opposite transfer in different regions, generalizing the principle from sample overlap to regional support-validity.
- Contribution stack:
  1. **Phenomenon**: define and measure transfer sign heterogeneity (pair-averaged and task-specific)
  2. **Failure mode**: characterize global affinity cancellation
  3. **Diagnostic**: region-conditional gradient affinity with explicit support-validity, evaluated against chemistry baselines
  4. **Utility**: local-affinity sharing reduces negative transfer
  5. **Robustness**: phenomenon holds across architectures, partitionings, and sample sizes
- Field-level implication: task compatibility is sign-changing, support-dependent, and region-conditional. Single-scalar task affinity is sufficient only when transfer is regionally stable.

## Part 14: Honest Bottom Line

The contribution is the claim stack, not the components. Individually, gradient affinity is not new, scaffolds are not new, regional analysis is not new, MTL is not new. The contribution is the field-level claim that task affinity should not be treated as a scalar in domains where transfer is regionally heterogeneous, supported by a phenomenon (sign heterogeneity), a failure mode (cancellation), a diagnostic (region-conditional gradient affinity), and a utility demonstration (local sharing decisions).

**Simplified implementation (recommended):**

Train task pairs separately, with regional masking:

For each task pair $(i, j)$:
- For each region $r$: set $\gamma_{ij}(r)$ based on $G_{ij}(r)$ pre-computed from short pretraining.
- During training, for each batch: for compounds in regions where $\gamma_{ij}(r) = 0$, the cross-task gradient contribution is zeroed.
- This is implemented by selectively masking the loss contribution of task $j$ on compounds in regions where the pair is "separated."

**Specifically, for hard sharing with task pair $(i, j)$:**

```
For each batch:
  For each compound x with valid label for task i:
    If π(x) = r and γ_ij(r) = 1: contribute to L_i (with shared encoder gradients)
    If π(x) = r and γ_ij(r) = 0: contribute to L_i^isolated (with isolated head; encoder treated as fixed for this loss term)
  Similarly for task j.
```

The "isolated head" trick: maintain a second copy of the task head $\phi_i^{\text{iso}}$ used only for compounds in separated regions. This head receives gradients but does not propagate them to the encoder.

**Gate setting:**
- Hard sharing: $\gamma_{ij}(r) = 1$ if $G_{ij}(r) > \tau_+$, else 0. Default $\tau_+ = 0$ (share if cosine is positive).
- Soft sharing: $\gamma_{ij}(r) = \sigma(\alpha \cdot G_{ij}(r))$. Default $\alpha = 5$.
- Abstention: $\gamma_{ij}(r) = 0$ AND task $j$ predictions in region $r$ are taken from STL model. For low-support regions (failing Section 2.11 validity), abstain by default.

**Pre-training for gate values:**

Run 5 epochs of standard pairwise MTL training with $\gamma = 1$ everywhere. Compute $G_{ij}(r)$ from the resulting model. Set gates. Then train for the remaining epochs with the gates active. Save final checkpoint.

**Implementation:** `architecture/local_affinity_sharing.py`. Include unit tests verifying gradient flow matches the gating specification.

### 2.16 Baseline implementations

All baselines use the same encoder architecture, optimizer, and training schedule unless noted. Implementations must be faithful; bad implementations are worse than no implementation.

**STL:** Section 2.4.

**All-task MTL:** Single shared encoder, $K$ task heads, joint training on all tasks simultaneously. Loss is sum over per-task losses with masking for missing labels.

**Pairwise MTL union:** Train pairwise MTL for each pair (Section 2.5). At test time, ensemble predictions for task $k$ across all pairs containing $k$ via averaging logits.

**Random grouping:** For $G$ groups, randomly assign each task to a group (10 trials averaged). Train all-task MTL within each group. Tasks in different groups do not share parameters.

**Global gradient affinity grouping (ICLR paper):** Compute $G_{ij}^{\text{global}}$ for all pairs from an all-task MTL pretrained model. Hierarchically cluster tasks using $1 - G_{ij}^{\text{global}}$ as distance. Cut dendrogram to produce $G$ groups. Train all-task MTL within each group.

**TAG-style affinity grouping:** Compute task affinity as $a_{i \to j} = \text{Perf}_j(\text{params after task } i \text{ update}) - \text{Perf}_j(\text{params before update})$ using the formulation from Fifty et al. Cluster tasks based on $a$.

**MMoE:** Implementation following Ma et al. 2018. Architecture:
- Shared expert tower: 4 experts, each is a 2-layer MLP with hidden dim 256
- Per-task gating network: input is encoder representation, output is softmax over 4 experts
- Per-task head on top of gated expert mixture
- Joint training across all tasks

**Adaptive sharing baseline:** Cross-Stitch Networks (Misra et al. 2016) is the primary recommendation due to implementation tractability. Architecture:
- Per-task encoder columns with shared cross-stitch units between layers
- Cross-stitch units are 2x2 learned matrices that mix per-task layer activations
- Trained jointly with sum-of-task-losses

If DynaShare implementation is achievable to reasonable fidelity within engineering budget, use it; otherwise Cross-Stitch.

**Implementation paths:**
- `baselines/stl.py`
- `baselines/all_task_mtl.py`
- `baselines/pairwise_union.py`
- `baselines/random_grouping.py`
- `baselines/global_affinity_grouping.py`
- `baselines/tag_grouping.py`
- `baselines/mmoe.py`
- `baselines/cross_stitch.py` (or `dynashare.py`)

Each baseline is independently testable: a unit test checks training converges and predictions are non-trivial on a 2-task synthetic problem.

### 2.17 Evaluation metrics for utility experiments

For each method and dataset:

**Average performance:**
- Per-task AUC (or negative RMSE), averaged across tasks
- Per-task macro-averaged across regions

**Worst-case performance:**
- Worst-region per-task performance (per task, take min over regions; then average across tasks)
- Worst-task overall performance

**Negative transfer rate:**
- $\text{NT-Rate} = \frac{\#\{(k, r) : \text{Perf}_k^{\text{method}}(r) < \text{Perf}_k^{\text{STL}}(r) - 1.5\}}{\#\{(k, r) : (k, r) \text{ valid}\}}$
- Severe NT rate: same with threshold 3.0 AUC

**Calibration:**
- For methods that produce share/separate decisions (local affinity, global affinity grouping, TAG): compute ECE on these decisions vs. ground-truth $\text{sign}(\Delta_{ij}(r))$
- Reliability diagram with 10 bins
- Brier score

**Held-out task-pair calibration:** 5-fold leave-task-pairs-out CV: train decision rule on 80% of pairs, evaluate calibration of decisions on 20%.

**Statistical comparison:** For each pair of methods, compute paired difference per (task, region). Wilcoxon signed-rank test. Bonferroni correction across $\binom{n_{\text{methods}}}{2}$ comparisons.

**Implementation:** `eval/utility_metrics.py`.

### 2.20 Decomposition of global affinity (analysis)

To explicitly link the regional and global views:

$G_{ij}^{\text{global}} = \cos\left(\sum_r p_i(r) g_i(r),\ \sum_r p_j(r) g_j(r)\right)$

This is a nonlinear function of regional gradients due to the cosine. We compute and report:

1. The actual global affinity $G_{ij}^{\text{global}}$
2. The naive linear approximation $\bar{G}_{ij} = \sum_r p(r) G_{ij}(r)$
3. The cancellation gap $|G_{ij}^{\text{global}} - \bar{G}_{ij}|$

For sign-heterogeneous pairs, $G_{ij}^{\text{global}}$ can be near zero while $|G_{ij}(r)|$ is large for individual regions — the cosine of summed vectors cancels in a way that the sum of cosines does not. Document this explicitly in the paper, with a worked example.

## Part 3: Risk Register and Mitigations

### Risk 1 (PRIMARY SCIENTIFIC RISK): Scaffold similarity explains everything

**Probability:** Medium-high. Two regions in the same scaffold cluster are by definition chemically similar; this might fully explain regional transfer patterns and reduce the gradient-affinity contribution to noise.

**Why this is the primary risk:** If true, the paper has no methodological contribution. The phenomenon would still exist, but the predictor's added value over a chemistry baseline would be zero, which would force a workshop paper.

**Detection:** In Phase 2, run joint regression $\Delta_{ij}(r) \sim \text{ScaffoldSim}_{ij}(r) + G_{ij}(r) + \text{LabelCorr}_{ij}(r)$ and report incremental $R^2$ and partial correlation of each predictor. This is a centerpiece result, not an aside.

**Mitigation:**
- Latent embedding partitioning as alternative (decouples chemical similarity from learned task structure)
- Within-scaffold-cluster analysis showing $G_{ij}(r)$ varies meaningfully even when scaffold structure is held constant
- Report incremental $R^2$ for $G_{ij}(r)$ over chemistry baselines as a primary result
- Consider that gradient affinity may capture *task-specific* representational alignment that scaffold similarity cannot, especially across diverse molecular families

**Kill criterion:** $G_{ij}(r)$ contributes less than $0.05$ incremental $R^2$ over scaffold similarity in joint regression, across multiple datasets. Reframe paper around scaffold-based regional structure rather than gradient affinity, and pivot to workshop submission.

### Risk 2: Sign heterogeneity is rare or absent

**Probability:** Medium. Plausible that MTL benefit is roughly monotonic across regions for most pairs.

**Detection:** Pilot Phase 0 directly measures prevalence.

**Mitigation:**
- Test deliberately weakly-related task pairs (cross-domain Tox21+ADME) where heterogeneity is more likely than for tightly-related pairs
- Test high-overlap datasets first to remove confounds
- Define meaningful effect sizes upfront (the 1.5 AUC threshold)
- Use sufficient region partitioning resolution (3-8 regions per dataset)
- Examine task-specific (asymmetric) heterogeneity as a separate channel — even pairs that look symmetric may show asymmetric regional transfer

**Kill criterion:** Fewer than 5 task pairs across all datasets show stable sign heterogeneity passing the CI test. Project becomes a workshop paper or is dropped.

### Risk 3: Local gradient affinity does not predict regional transfer sign

**Probability:** Medium-low. Your ICLR paper showed global cosine works above 30% overlap; locally restricted versions should also work given enough samples per region.

**Detection:** Phase 0 pilot measures regional sign prediction AUROC.

**Mitigation:**
- TAG-style affinity as backup metric (pre-registered as ablation)
- Last-shared-layer gradients vs. all-encoder gradients (tested as ablation)
- Fisher-normalized gradients (tested as ablation)
- Larger region sizes if small-region noise dominates

**Kill criterion:** Local gradient affinity AUROC for regional sign prediction does not exceed best non-gradient baseline (scaffold similarity, regional label correlation, embedding distance) by at least 0.07. Project drops to workshop paper.

### Risk 4: Reviewers see this as DynaShare/MMoE rebranded

**Probability:** Medium. The most likely reviewer attack.

**Mitigation:**
- Frame the contribution as diagnosis and measurement, not as a competing architecture
- Include MMoE and one other adaptive sharing baseline (Cross-Stitch, Sluice, or DynaShare if implementation is clean) as performance baselines
- Show calibrated regional sign prediction as something routing methods do not provide (their gating weights are not designed to predict regional MTL benefit)
- Demonstrate that local gradient affinity, computed before MTL training, predicts what routing methods learn during training. This positions local affinity as a *predictor* and *diagnostic*, not a competitor

### Risk 5: Region partitioning is itself a confound

**Probability:** Medium. Different partitionings might give different sign-heterogeneity prevalence.

**Mitigation:**
- Test all three partitioning schemes (scaffolds, latent clusters, kNN)
- Report agreement: phenomenon must hold across at least two
- Random-partition negative control with proper null distribution: scaffold-based prevalence must exceed 95th percentile of random-partition null
- Vary number of regions (3, 5, 8, 10) and report stability

### Risk 6: Small-region statistical noise

**Probability:** High. Some regions will have small samples even after the $n_{\min}$ threshold.

**Mitigation:**
- Hierarchical bootstrap CIs everywhere
- Abstention via the CI-width criterion
- Multiple seeds (5 final) and averaging
- Explicit noise-floor experiment: shuffle labels within regions and show $G_{ij}(r)$ becomes noise

### Risk 7: Compute cost

**Probability:** Medium. Pairwise MTL training across many task pairs, multiple seeds, multiple architectures is expensive.

**Mitigation:**
- 2-4 H100 GPUs per available tooling
- Reuse trained encoders where possible
- Pilot uses 3 seeds; final uses 5
- Limit final task-pair count to ~80-120 across all datasets
- Stage compute: don't run final architecture variants until pilot greenlights

### Risk 8: Phenomenon exists but is too dataset-specific

**Probability:** Medium-low. Phenomenon should generalize across molecular datasets, but might not generalize beyond chemistry.

**Mitigation:**
- Own the molecular scope explicitly. The paper is about sparse molecular MTL with chemical structure.
- Briefly speculate on generalizability in discussion without claiming it
- Future work section discusses extension to other domains

The unconditional NeurIPS probability is in the 12-18% range, which sounds low but reflects the compounding risk of pilot failure, scaffold-similarity confound, execution issues, and reviewer variance. If the pilot greenlights, the conditional probability rises substantially.

## Part 5: Phase 0 — Pilot

### 5.1 Goal

Decide whether the project is worth pursuing. Test the phenomenon claim and the predictor claim with minimal infrastructure.

### 5.2 Datasets

Tox21 (12 tasks, 100% overlap), SIDER (27 tasks, 100% overlap). High overlap removes the support-availability confound at this stage.

### 5.3 Task pairs

20-40 task pairs sampled to include:
- Within-mechanism pairs (e.g., NR vs. NR pathways in Tox21) — expected positive transfer
- Cross-mechanism pairs (NR vs. SR) — likely heterogeneous
- SOC-grouped vs. cross-SOC pairs in SIDER

### 5.4 Architecture

Reuse ICLR paper's GCN architecture: 3-layer GCN, hidden dim 256, global mean pooling, MLP heads. AdamW, lr 1e-3, batch 32, 30 epochs, early stopping on validation.

### 5.5 Region partitioning

Bemis-Murcko scaffolds. Cluster scaffolds into 3-5 groups using hierarchical clustering on Tanimoto similarity of scaffold fingerprints. Require minimum 50 compounds per region.

### 5.6 Experimental protocol

For each dataset:

1. Train STL models for each task. 3 seeds. Save checkpoints.
2. Train pairwise MTL models for each task pair. 3 seeds. Save checkpoints.
3. For each pair × region: compute $\Delta_{ij}(r)$, $\Delta_{i \leftarrow j}(r)$, $\Delta_{j \leftarrow i}(r)$ on test split with hierarchical-bootstrap 95% CI.
4. For each pair × region: compute $G_{ij}(r)$ from gradients on training compounds in region. Hierarchical-bootstrap CI on $G_{ij}(r)$.
5. For each pair: compute $S_{ij}$ (sign heterogeneity, pair and task-specific), $H_{ij}$ (heterogeneity index), $C_{ij}$ (cancellation).
6. Test predictor: across all valid regions, does $G_{ij}(r)$ predict $\text{sign}(\Delta_{ij}(r))$? Compute AUROC and Spearman.
7. Build random-partition null distributions (200 random partitions per dataset) and compare scaffold-based metrics against the null.
8. Filter task pairs by the four-criteria "meaningful pair" definition before computing prevalence.

### 5.7 Green-light criteria (BOTH required)

**Phenomenon check:**
- At least 5 task pairs across the two datasets show stable sign heterogeneity (passes $|\Delta| > 1.5$ AUC threshold AND CI test in at least one positive and one negative region)
- AND at least 15% of meaningful task pairs (per the four-criteria definition in Section 1.3) exhibit sign heterogeneity
- AND scaffold-based heterogeneity prevalence exceeds the 95th percentile of the random-partition null distribution

**Predictor check:**
- Local gradient affinity AUROC for regional sign prediction $\geq 0.70$
- AND local gradient affinity AUROC exceeds best non-gradient baseline (scaffold similarity, regional label correlation, embedding distance) by at least 0.07
- AND Spearman $\rho$ between $G_{ij}(r)$ and $\Delta_{ij}(r) \geq 0.45$
- AND results stable across 3 seeds (sign of $G_{ij}(r)$ agrees in $\geq 80\%$ of regions across seeds)

### 5.8 Decision tree

- **Both pass:** Full project proceed. NeurIPS target.
- **Phenomenon passes, predictor fails:** Workshop paper on phenomenon discovery; or invest in better predictors (TAG-style, Fisher-normalized) before deciding.
- **Phenomenon fails, predictor untestable:** Drop project or pivot to "conditions under which global affinity is sufficient" framing (much weaker, probably workshop only).
- **Both fail:** Drop project.

### 5.9 Pilot deliverables

- Sign heterogeneity prevalence table across datasets, with random-partition null comparison
- Pair-averaged AND task-specific regional benefit tables
- Predictor AUROC comparison vs. baselines
- One compelling task-pair example for Figure 2
- Hierarchical-bootstrap stability assessment
- Decision document with explicit go/no-go reasoning

## Part 7: Phase 2 — Predictive Evaluation

### 7.1 Goal

Show that local gradient affinity predicts regional transfer better than alternatives, and crucially that it adds value beyond chemistry baselines.

### 7.2 Prediction targets

For each (task pair, region) with valid local support:
- Binary: $\text{sign}(\Delta_{ij}(r))$
- Continuous: $\Delta_{ij}(r)$ value
- Three-class: positive transfer / negative transfer / no significant transfer
- Task-specific equivalents: $\text{sign}(\Delta_{i \leftarrow j}(r))$ and $\text{sign}(\Delta_{j \leftarrow i}(r))$

### 7.3 Predictor methods

**Primary methods:**
1. Local gradient cosine: $G_{ij}(r)$
2. Local gradient cosine + local support size as features (small classifier)
3. TAG-style local affinity: effect of one task's regional gradient step on the other task's regional loss

**Non-gradient baselines:**
4. Scaffold similarity: mean Tanimoto similarity between $D_i(r)$ and $D_j(r)$ scaffolds
5. Regional embedding distance: $\|\text{mean}(\phi(D_i(r))) - \text{mean}(\phi(D_j(r)))\|$
6. Regional label correlation: Pearson correlation of labels for compounds with both labels in $r$ (when available)
7. Regional MMD between task input distributions
8. Global gradient affinity: $G_{ij}^{\text{global}}$ from your ICLR paper, applied to all regions of a pair (constant predictor)
9. Regional Task2Vec embedding similarity (if computationally feasible)

**Joint predictor (centerpiece for primary risk):**
10. Logistic regression: $\text{sign}(\Delta_{ij}(r)) \sim G_{ij}(r) + \text{ScaffoldSim} + \text{LabelCorr} + \text{EmbeddingDist}$ — used to compute incremental contribution of $G_{ij}(r)$ over chemistry baselines

### 7.4 Metrics

- AUROC for sign prediction (pair-averaged AND task-specific)
- AUPRC
- Balanced accuracy
- Spearman $\rho$ with $\Delta_{ij}(r)$
- Calibration (reliability diagram, ECE)
- Hierarchical-bootstrap CIs on all metrics
- **Incremental $R^2$ of $G_{ij}(r)$ in joint regression — primary scientific result**

### 7.5 Cross-dataset transfer

Train predictor on Tox21, evaluate on SIDER. Train on SIDER, evaluate on ToxCast. Tests whether the relationship between local gradient affinity and regional transfer generalizes across datasets.

### 7.6 Phase 2 deliverables

- Main predictor comparison table (Figure 5)
- Scatter plot of $G_{ij}(r)$ vs. $\Delta_{ij}(r)$ across all datasets (Figure 4)
- Incremental $R^2$ figure showing $G_{ij}(r)$ adds predictive value beyond chemistry baselines (Figure 5b — centerpiece for primary risk)
- Calibration plot for top predictors
- Cross-dataset transfer results

## Part 8: Phase 3 — Utility Evaluation

This phase establishes practical value: using local affinity to make sharing decisions. Real architectures, real baselines, honest comparison.

### 8.1 Goal

Show that local-affinity-guided sharing reduces regional negative transfer while preserving regional positive transfer, and either matches or beats existing routing methods.

### 8.2 Sharing strategies compared

**Single-task baselines:**
1. STL: train each task independently

**Static all-task baselines:**
2. All-task MTL: train one shared encoder on all tasks
3. Pairwise MTL union: train pairwise MTL for each pair, ensemble at test

**Global grouping baselines:**
4. Random grouping (10 trials averaged)
5. Global gradient affinity grouping (your ICLR paper)
6. TAG-style affinity grouping (Fifty et al. or close re-implementation)

**Adaptive sharing baselines:**
7. MMoE: mixture-of-experts with task-specific gates
8. One additional adaptive sharing method: DynaShare if implementation is clean and faithful, otherwise Cross-Stitch Networks (Misra et al.) or Sluice Networks (Ruder et al.). Decision made before Phase 3 begins based on engineering assessment. Bad implementation is worse than no implementation; the baseline must be defensible.

**Local affinity strategies (the proposed approach):**
9. Local affinity hard sharing: For each region, pre-compute $G_{ij}(r)$ for all task pairs. Train region-conditional MTL where shared parameters are active only between task pairs with $G_{ij}(r) > \tau_+$ in that region.
10. Local affinity soft sharing: Use $G_{ij}(r)$ as a continuous gate weight on cross-task parameter sharing per region.
11. Local affinity with abstention: Like (9), but for low-support regions, default to STL prediction.

### 8.3 Implementation of local affinity sharing architecture

The proposed architecture must be implemented carefully. One reasonable approach:

- Shared encoder produces representation $\phi(x)$
- Region assignment function $r(x)$ assigns each compound to a region (scaffold-based, deterministic)
- Task heads receive $\phi(x)$ plus region identity
- For each task pair $(i, j)$ and region $r$, a learned binary gate $\gamma_{ij}(r)$ controls whether encoder gradients from task $i$ flow back through shared parameters when task $j$ is being trained on region $r$
- $\gamma_{ij}(r)$ is initialized using $G_{ij}(r)$ pre-computed from a short pretraining run, then optionally fine-tuned

This is a significant engineering effort. The paper includes a clean public implementation.

### 8.4 Datasets

- Tox21, SIDER, ToxCast (high overlap)
- TDC ADMET, kinase panel (sparse)

### 8.5 Metrics

**Performance metrics:**
- Average per-task AUC/RMSE (across tasks)
- Worst-region performance (per task, then averaged)
- Average regional performance
- Sample-weighted average performance

**Negative transfer metrics:**
- Negative transfer rate: fraction of (task, region) pairs where method underperforms STL by at least 1.5 AUC
- Severe negative transfer rate: underperforms by at least 3 AUC
- Average regional negative transfer magnitude
- Task-specific negative transfer rate (using $\Delta_{i \leftarrow j}$ rather than pair average)

**Decision-quality metrics:**
- Calibration of share/separate decisions: when method predicts "share is beneficial," how often is it correct?
- Reliability diagrams for share/separate predictions
- Expected calibration error (ECE) on share/separate decisions
- Held-out task-pair calibration: fit decision rule on training task pairs, evaluate calibration on held-out pairs
- AUROC of sharing decisions vs. ground-truth regional benefit

**Efficiency metrics:**
- Training compute (GPU-hours)
- Inference latency
- Number of effective parameters

### 8.6 Statistical comparison

Pairwise comparisons between methods using paired tests across (task, region) combinations. Bonferroni correction across the comparison family. Hierarchical-bootstrap CIs on all reported numbers.

### 8.7 Expected outcomes and pre-committed interpretation

- If local affinity sharing matches or beats DynaShare/MMoE on average performance AND has substantially lower negative transfer rate: strong result
- If local affinity sharing matches DynaShare/MMoE on average but is interpretable while they are not: moderate result, framed around interpretability and calibration
- If local affinity sharing substantially underperforms DynaShare/MMoE on average: still publishable as a diagnostic, but the contribution shifts

The paper must commit to honesty here. If routing methods beat local affinity on raw performance, the paper says so and reframes the contribution around regional diagnosis and calibrated abstention rather than claiming SOTA.

### 8.8 Phase 3 deliverables

- Main performance table across all methods and datasets
- Negative transfer rate comparison (key figure)
- Calibration analysis with reliability diagrams
- Per-region performance breakdown for selected task pairs
- Public implementation of local-affinity sharing architecture

## Part 10: Phase 5 — Theory and Discussion

### 10.1 Theoretical contributions

The paper does not claim a major theorem but includes:

- A formal definition of region-conditional task affinity
- A statement (with proof sketch in appendix) that under structured covariate shift across regions, the global gradient affinity is a function of (a) regional affinities, (b) regional sample weights, (c) the cosine geometry — and is not in general a weighted average of regional affinities
- A signal-to-noise analysis of when $G_{ij}(r)$ is a reliable estimator: depends on per-region sample size, gradient norm, and noise structure
- Conditions under which local-affinity-guided sharing is provably beneficial in expectation (under simplified assumptions)

These are framed as supporting analysis, not primary contributions. The paper is empirical.

### 10.2 Connection to ICLR paper

Explicit section showing the ICLR paper's overlap requirement is a special case. When tasks have full overlap (Tox21, SIDER), the global gradient affinity is reliable because all compounds are shared. When overlap is partial, regions of the input space have asymmetric task coverage, and global affinity becomes a function of which regions happen to be sampled. The NeurIPS paper generalizes the ICLR result by showing that even at full overlap, regional structure can make global affinity misleading. We generalize the principle from global sample overlap to regional support-validity.

### 10.3 When global affinity is sufficient

The paper explicitly identifies regimes where global affinity is fine:
- High-overlap datasets where all task pairs have positive (or all negative) transfer everywhere
- Task pairs where regional effects are roughly uniform
- Coarse-grained planning where regional decisions are not actionable

This honest framing strengthens the paper.

### 10.4 Limitations

- Molecular focus: extension to vision/NLP is future work
- Region partitioning is itself a modeling choice; the paper doesn't fully solve "what is the right region structure"
- Local-affinity sharing architecture has computational overhead vs. all-task MTL
- Regional sign prediction requires sufficient per-region samples; very sparse settings remain challenging
- Hierarchical bootstrap assumes scaffold groupings have approximately exchangeable substructure within clusters

## Part 12: Paper Structure

### 12.1 Abstract

Multi-task learning methods typically treat task compatibility as a global relationship between task pairs. We show this assumption can fail: the same pair of molecular prediction tasks can exhibit positive transfer in one chemical region and negative transfer in another. This *transfer sign heterogeneity* causes global task-affinity scores to cancel opposing regional effects and mislead task grouping. We define region-conditional transfer benefit, introduce support-valid regional gradient affinity as a diagnostic, and show that local gradient affinity predicts regional transfer beyond scaffold similarity and label-correlation baselines. Across five molecular datasets, sign heterogeneity is common (X% of meaningful task pairs, exceeding random-partition null at p<0.05) and produces measurable cancellation in global affinity. Local gradient affinity predicts regional transfer sign with AUROC Y, adding incremental $R^2$ of Z over chemistry baselines. Using local affinity for share/separate/abstain decisions reduces regional negative transfer by W% while matching adaptive sharing on average performance. Our results suggest task affinity should not be treated as a scalar relationship in domains where transfer is regionally heterogeneous.

### 12.2 Introduction (1.5 pages)

Lead with the phenomenon, not the method:

- MTL needs task compatibility decisions. Existing methods (TAG, DynaShare, MMoE) provide task-pair-level affinity scores or implicit instance-level routing.
- These methods share an unstated assumption: task compatibility is a property of the task pair. We show this assumption can fail.
- Concrete example: introduce one canonical sign-heterogeneous task pair from results, with the regional benefit figure inline.
- Generalization: across five datasets, this happens often enough that global task affinity is systematically misleading.
- The ICLR paper showed gradient affinity requires shared samples. We show that even with shared samples, the same pair can show opposite transfer in different regions, generalizing the principle from sample overlap to regional support-validity.
- Contribution stack:
  1. **Phenomenon**: define and measure transfer sign heterogeneity (pair-averaged and task-specific)
  2. **Failure mode**: characterize global affinity cancellation
  3. **Diagnostic**: region-conditional gradient affinity with explicit support-validity, evaluated against chemistry baselines
  4. **Utility**: local-affinity sharing reduces negative transfer
  5. **Robustness**: phenomenon holds across architectures, partitionings, and sample sizes
- Field-level implication: task compatibility is sign-changing, support-dependent, and region-conditional. Single-scalar task affinity is sufficient only when transfer is regionally stable.

### 13.1 Pilot decision (after Phase 0)

If both green-light criteria pass: proceed with full project, NeurIPS target.
If only phenomenon passes: workshop paper or invest in better predictors.
If only predictor passes: unusual; investigate region partitioning.
If neither passes: drop project or pivot to weaker framing.

### 13.2 Mid-project decision (after Phase 2)

If predictive evaluation works strongly across datasets AND $G_{ij}(r)$ adds incremental $R^2 \geq 0.05$ over chemistry baselines: continue to utility experiments.
If predictive evaluation is dataset-dependent: scope down to demonstrated datasets, write paper accordingly.
If incremental $R^2$ test fails (scaffold similarity dominates): kill project as NeurIPS candidate, reframe as workshop discovery paper.

### 13.3 Pre-submission decision (after Phase 3)

If utility experiments show clear negative transfer reduction: NeurIPS submission.
If utility experiments match but don't beat MMoE/adaptive baseline on average: NeurIPS submission with framing around regional diagnosis and calibration.
If utility experiments substantially underperform: ICML or workshop submission with phenomenon-focused framing.

### 13.4 Final claims

The paper claims, in order of strength:

1. Transfer sign heterogeneity exists and is common in molecular MTL, both pair-averaged and task-specific
2. Global affinity averages over regional effects and is misleading for sign-heterogeneous pairs (cancellation phenomenon)
3. Region-conditional gradient affinity, computed with explicit support-validity, predicts regional transfer with measurable incremental value over chemistry baselines
4. Local-affinity-guided sharing reduces negative transfer while maintaining average performance
5. The phenomenon is robust to partitioning, architecture, and sample size choices, and exceeds random-partition null distributions

The paper does not claim:
- Beating routing methods on average performance (unless it does)
- Generalization beyond molecules
- A complete theoretical characterization
- Novel sample-overlap requirement (generalized from ICLR)

## Part 14: Honest Bottom Line

The contribution is the claim stack, not the components. Individually, gradient affinity is not new, scaffolds are not new, regional analysis is not new, MTL is not new. The contribution is the field-level claim that task affinity should not be treated as a scalar in domains where transfer is regionally heterogeneous, supported by a phenomenon (sign heterogeneity), a failure mode (cancellation), a diagnostic (region-conditional gradient affinity), and a utility demonstration (local sharing decisions).

This framing is what makes the project a NeurIPS candidate rather than an incremental MTL method paper. It's also what makes scoop accusations harder: no prior paper has made this specific claim stack, even though the components are all in the literature.

The pilot is the gating decision. Conditional on the pilot passing both checks AND scaffold similarity not explaining everything, this is a 35-45% NeurIPS main candidate. The probability is what it is regardless of how well the contribution is framed; reviewers respond to evidence, not slogans. But framing determines whether reviewers grasp the contribution quickly enough to evaluate the evidence on its merits, so the phenomenon-first framing matters operationally even if it doesn't shift acceptance odds dramatically.

The single biggest scientific risk is that scaffold similarity explains everything and gradient affinity adds no incremental value. The incremental-$R^2$ test in Phase 2 is the deciding experiment for that question, and the paper's centerpiece result.

The single biggest execution risk is the utility experiment in Phase 3, because the local-affinity sharing architecture is a significant engineering effort and adaptive sharing baselines (MMoE plus DynaShare/Cross-Stitch/Sluice) require honest implementation.

If both risks are managed and the pilot greenlights, this is a real paper.
